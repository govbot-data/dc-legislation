Dear Secretary Smith,
I am introducing the “Philip Reed Memorial Park Designation Act of 2025”. Please see enclosed
signed copy of the legislation.
This legislation will symbolically designate a parcel of land located in Woodridge South as the
Philip Reed Memorial Park. I am proud to introduce this legislation with the support and
advocacy of the Woodridge Civic Association.
Born Philip “Reid,” Reid was an enslaved man who was a part of the U.S. Capitol’s construction
in the 1860s. Reid was an enslaved laborer for sculptor Clark Mills, who cast the Statue of
Freedom which now sits atop the U.S. Capitol Dome. Reid was the only documented enslaved
laborer to work on the Statue of Freedom, a nineteen-foot tall bronze statue. Philip Reid worked
seven days a week, and was able to receive pay for his work done on Sundays over the course of
at least nine months. During the process of casting the Statue, the plaster model of the Statue
needed to be separated, and Philip Reid discovered the process to successfully separate it for
transport and casting.
Philip Reid was emancipated on April 16, 1862 by the District of Columbia Compensated
Emancipation Act, which released certain persons in service or labor positions in the District of
Columbia. Philip Reid formally changed his name to Philip Reed upon emancipation. Reed
became a noted, well-regarded plasterer in his own right and took up residence here in the
District of Columbia. As noted by the White House Historical Association, the irony of an
enslaved man working on the Statue of Freedom was not lost on the nation, and a New York
Tribune reporter writing about the construction project asked: “Was there a prophecy in that

moment when the slave became the artist, and with rare poetic justice, reconstructed the beautiful
symbol of freedom for America?”1
Please feel free to reach out to me or my Legislative Director, Neferteria Brown, with any
questions or for additional information. She can be reached at nbrown@dccouncil.gov or by
phone at (771) 333-9734.
