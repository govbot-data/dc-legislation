Statement of Introduction
Institution of Higher Education Sexual Misconduct Reporting and
Resource Accessibility Act of 2025
January 6, 2025
Today, I am introducing the Institution of Higher Education Sexual Misconduct Reporting and
Resource Accessibility Act of 2025, along with Councilmembers Janeese Lewis George, Robert
C. White, Jr., Charles Allen, Matthew Frumin, Brooke Pinto, and Brianne K. Nadeau. This
measure was first introduced in Council Period 25.
Students attending college in the District should be certain of accruing benefits such as job
security, exceptional networking opportunities, high earnings, and personal development, but for
some students, experiencing sexual misconduct during their educational career is also highly
likely.
Sexual misconduct is a prevalent crime against women, transgender people, genderqueer people,
and nonbinary people at institutions of higher education. According to the American Association
of Universities’ 2020 campus climate survey on sexual assault and sexual misconduct,
nationally, 59% of undergraduate women reported experiencing harassing behavior since
enrolling at school, and 23% of transgender, genderqueer, and nonbinary students experienced
unwanted sexual contact.1 Less than half of students surveyed who had experienced sexual
misconduct thought it likely that an official at a school would take an allegation of sexual
misconduct seriously. Only 29.5% of victims contacted at least one program or resource to
support them, but only 20.6% of these contacted Title IX and 17.9% contacted their campus
victim services office. Institutions of higher education are required to publicly report reported
incidents of sexual violence, but low survivor reporting leads to low institutional reporting. Only
one third of students felt they were very or extremely knowledgeable about the definition of
sexual assault, where to get help, and how to report it, which underscores the importance of
training so students understand what sexual assault is and how to get help. It is important that a
student survivor feel confident in the resources and processes that are available to them through
their school during one of the worst times of their life.
While the District continues to work toward the goal of preventing sexual assault and providing
quality resources for survivors, it is equally important to sharpen the tools at our disposal that

This legislation would further the District’s commitment to supporting survivors of sexual
misconduct by:
§ Requiring Institutions of higher education in the District to retain a confidential resource
advisor (CRA) to provide emergency and ongoing support to reporting parties at
institutions of higher education.
o The CRA would be required to be experienced in sexual misconduct work,
disseminate information to reporting parties and to institution staff and students.
o A CRA would provide victims with assistance regarding navigating processes in
the institution, in law enforcement, being connected to resources following an
experience of sexual misconduct, and other supports the victim requests.
o All communications between the reporting party and the CRA would be
confidential, and the reporting party would have to approve the CRA
communicating with outside entities in most circumstances.
o This section of the bill also creates flexibility for small institutions by allowing
them to partner with another institution or enter into a Memorandum of
Understanding (MOU) with a local victim advocacy organization to provide these
services.
o In the District, George Washington University, American University, and Howard
University currently have CRAs.
§ Requiring amnesty protections for reporting parties of sexual misconduct for violations of
an institution’s student conduct policy if the violation occurred immediately before,
during, or immediately after the incident of sexual misconduct, unless the violation put at
immediate and severe risk the health and safety of others.
§ Requiring institutions to facilitate mandatory annual sexual misconduct trainings for all
students with information about consent, the impact of drugs and alcohol on an
individual’s ability to consent, procedures following sexual misconduct, contact
information for the CRA, bystander intervention strategies, the rights of reporting parties,
and the institution’s responsibilities regarding no-contact orders, orders of protection, or
restraining orders.
§ Requiring institution of higher education personnel who are involved in an institution of
higher education's investigative and disciplinary process regarding sexual misconduct to
receive annual training in handling sexual misconduct complaints and in the operations of
the institution's disciplinary process.
§ Requiring that CRAs, Title IX coordinators, and an institution of higher education’s
public safety personnel be educated in trauma-informed responses, and investigators to
receive annual training on conducting investigations using trauma-informed responses.
§ Establishing minimum criteria for institutions’ sexual misconduct policies.
§ Allowing institutions to grant students who report experiencing sexual misconduct to
specific institution personnel a waiver from minimum GPA, credit hour load, or other
academic requirements or disciplinary record standards if the reporting student consents

Sexual misconduct is uprooted by safe, trustworthy, and transparent environments where
institutions’ sexual misconduct policies are informed by recent and relevant data, where
reporting parties can access information and resources from a confidential source present solely
to provide support, where the privacy of reporting parties is respected, and where staff are
regularly trained to respond to sexual misconduct. The Council is committed to ensuring safe
educational environments for students and personnel at the District’s institutions of higher
education and to ensuring that our institutions empower survivors to seek resources following
any incident of sexual misconduct, and I look forward to working with my colleagues to realize
these goals.

49 Sec. 2. Definitions.
50 For the purposes of this act, the term:
51 (1) “Institution of higher education” or “institution” shall have the same meaning
52 as provided in section 101 of the Higher Education Act (20 U.S.C. §1001).
53 (2) “Reporting party” means a student or employee of an institution who reports
54 having experienced an incident of sexual misconduct to the institution.
55 (3) “Responding party” means a student, employee, or other individual who has
56 been accused of an incident of sexual misconduct by a reporting party.
57 (4) “Student” means an individual who is enrolled at least part-time in a credit-
58 bearing program through a public or private degree-granting institution of higher education.
59 (5) “Sexual misconduct” shall have the same meaning as the following terms:
60 (A) Dating violence, domestic violence, and stalking, as defined in the
61 Consolidated Appropriations Act of 2022 (Pub. L. 117-103, 136 Stat. 49);
62 (B) Sexual assault, as defined in the Jeanne Clery Disclosure of Campus
63 Security Policy and Campus Crime Statistics Act (20 USC § 1092); or
64 (C) Sexual harassment, as defined in Title IX of the Education
65 Amendments of 1972 (34 CFR 106.30(a)).
66 (6) “Title IX coordinator” means an employee designated by an institution whose
67 responsibilities include those required by 34 C.F.R. § 106.8(a).
2

68 (7) “Trauma-informed response" means a response involving an understanding of
69 the complexities of sexual misconduct through training centered on the neurobiological impact of
70 trauma, the influence of myths and stereotypes surrounding the causes and impacts of trauma,
71 perpetration methodology, and methods to conduct investigations.
72 Sec. 3. Confidential resource advisor role and responsibilities.
73 (a) Each institution shall designate a confidential resource advisor who shall provide
74 emergency and ongoing support to reporting parties at institutions of higher education in the
75 District as described in this section. Each institution shall also designate an employee who has
76 received training which conforms with the requirements of subsection (b)(3) of this section to
77 fulfill the responsibilities of the confidential resource advisor if necessary.
78 (b) The confidential resource advisor:
79 (1) Shall:
80 (A) Be appointed based on the individual’s professional experience and
81 demonstrated ability to effectively provide victim services related to sexual misconduct;
82 (B) Provide written notice to all institution staff involved in providing or
83 enforcing relevant supportive measures or accommodations of the requirements of this section;
84 (C) Shall complete yearly trainings in sexual misconduct awareness and
85 prevention, Title IX policies, in trauma-informed response, and, if employed by an institution,
86 shall complete yearly trainings in the institution of higher education’s policies pertaining to
87 student conduct, harassment, and sexual relationships; and
88 (D) Shall establish communication with sexual misconduct resource
89 providers in the District and at the institution within 3 months of being designated as a
90 confidential resource advisor.

92 (A) Act as a counselor or therapist;
93 (B) Disclose a confidential communication as defined by § 14-310 except:

96 (iii) To other individuals employed at the institution or others and
97 third party providers when and to the extent necessary to facilitate the delivery of services to the
98 victim;
99 (iv) To the Metropolitan Police Department or other law
100 enforcement agency to the extent necessary to protect the victim or another individual from a
101 substantial risk of imminent and serious physical injury;
102 (v) To compile statistical or anecdotal information, without

104 (vi) For any confidential communications relevant to a claim or

106 program;
107 (C) Provide services to the reporting party and responding party of the
108 same incident;
109 (D) Unless otherwise required to do so by state or federal law, be required
110 to report an incident of sexual misconduct to the institution or to a law enforcement agency;
111 (E) Be a student at the institution of higher education at which the
112 confidential resource advisor is employed;

201 Sec. 4. Amnesty protections for reporting parties.
8

202 (a) A reporting party or a witness to an incident of sexual misconduct who reports an
203 incident of sexual misconduct to an institution or local or federal law enforcement shall not be
204 subject to a disciplinary proceeding or sanction for a violation of the institution of higher
205 education's student conduct policy that occurred immediately preceding, during, or immediately
206 following the incident of sexual misconduct unless the institution determines that the report was
207 not made in good faith or that the violation put at immediate and severe risk the health and safety
208 of other individuals.
209 (b) Notwithstanding an institution’s code of conduct, an institution shall not take
210 disciplinary action against reporting parties for consensual sexual activity related to the incident
211 or other consensual sexual activity revealed during an investigation into the reported incident.
212 Sec. 5. Annual sexual misconduct awareness training.
213 (a) An institution of higher education shall provide mandatory annual sexual misconduct
214 primary prevention and awareness programming that is trauma-informed and gender-inclusive
215 for all students of the institution. The training shall be mandatory and shall include:
216 (1) An explanation of consent in reference to sexual activity and sexual
217 relationships;
218 (2) The role that drugs and alcohol play in an individual's ability to consent;
219 (3) Procedures victims should follow if sexual misconduct has occurred, including
220 information in writing about:
221 (i) The importance of preserving evidence to document sexual
222 misconduct;
223 (ii) Procedures to report an incident of sexual misconduct, including
224 confidential and anonymous disclosure; and
9

225 (iii) Information on the institution's procedures for resolving sexual
226 misconduct complaints and the range of sanctions or penalties the institution may impose on
227 students and employees found responsible for a violation;
228 (4) The name, contact information, and role of the confidential resource advisor;
229 (5) Safe and positive strategies for bystander intervention that may be carried out
230 by an individual to prevent harm or reduce risk;
231 (6) The rights of reporting parties and the institution’s responsibilities regarding
232 orders of protection, no contact orders, restraining orders, or similar lawful orders issued by a
233 criminal or civil court; and
234 (7) Opportunities for ongoing sexual misconduct prevention and sexual
235 misconduct awareness training and programming.
236 (b) Trainings shall be culturally responsive and reflect students’ experiences, including
237 race, color, ethnicity, national origin, religion, economic status, disability, sexual orientation,
238 gender identity, and reproductive status.
239 Sec. 6. Training for individuals involved in sexual misconduct response processes.
240 (a) An individual who participates in the implementation of an institution of higher
241 education's investigative and disciplinary process, including an individual responsible for
242 resolving complaints of reported incidents, an individual responsible for conducting a meeting,
243 hearing or other disciplinary proceeding or informal resolution process, and the confidential
244 resource advisor employed by the institution shall have annual training in handling sexual
245 misconduct complaints and in the operations of the institution's disciplinary process, which shall
246 include:
10

247 (1) Information about working with and interviewing individuals who have been
248 subjected to sexual misconduct;
249 (2) Information about types of conduct that constitute sexual misconduct;
250 (3) Information about consent and the role drugs and alcohol may play in an
251 individual's ability to consent;
252 (3) The effects of trauma, including the manifestation of neurobiological and
253 physical impact on an individual;
254 (4) Cultural competence training regarding the impacts of sexual misconduct
255 depending on factors that contribute to an individual’s cultural background, including race, color,
256 national origin, ethnicity, religion, economic status, gender identity, gender expression, sexual
257 orientation, pregnancy, and parenting status;
258 (5) Techniques to communicate sensitively and compassionately with a reporting
259 party, including responding to a reporting party with consideration of that party's background;
260 (6) Techniques to communicate sensitively with a responding party, including an
261 awareness of the emotional impact of being wrongly accused; and
262 (7) Training and information regarding how sexual misconduct may impact
263 students with disabilities.
264 Sec. 7. Institution personnel trauma-informed training requirements.
265 (a) Each institution of higher education shall ensure that its confidential resource advisor,
266 Title IX office personnel, and public safety personnel employed by the institution are educated in
267 trauma-informed responses.
11

268 (b) Each institution of higher education shall ensure that its investigators receive annual
269 training on conducting an investigation using trauma-informed responses and which protects the
270 safety of victims and promotes accountability.
271 Sec. 8. Minimum criteria for institutional sexual misconduct policies.
272 (a) Institutions of higher education in the District of Columbia shall ensure that the
273 institution’s sexual misconduct policy includes:
274 (1) Information on where victims of sexual assault can receive immediate
275 emergency assistance following an alleged incident of sexual misconduct, which shall include:
276 (A) The name and location of medical facilities where an individual may
277 request that a medical forensic exam be administered by a trained sexual assault forensic health
278 care provider;
279 (B) Descriptions and contact information for local rape crisis centers and
280 domestic violence resource providers, and a description of the services provided by such centers;
281 (C) The telephone number and website for a national 24-hour sexual
282 assault hotline;
283 (D) Information on any programs that may provide financial assistance to
284 a student with the cost of emergency medical assistance or related travel; and
285 (E) Information regarding the legal and institutional obligations of the
286 Title IX coordinator and employees of the Title IX office; and
287 (2) Descriptions of the roles and responsibilities and contact information for the
288 confidential resource advisor and the institution's Title IX coordinator.
12

289 (b) Each institution of higher education shall distribute the information required under
290 subsection (a) by electronic or postal mail to students and employees of the institution, or shall
291 maintain the information on its website.
292 Sec. 9. Waivers for student reporting parties.
293 (a) Institutions may grant to a student who has reported an experience of sexual assault to
294 the institution’s confidential resource advisor, Title IX coordinator, public safety personnel, or
295 local law enforcement and has given written consent to those individuals to confirm to the
296 institution the incident of sexual misconduct, a waiver from requirements that the student
297 maintain a minimum grade point average, credit hour load, or satisfy other academic
298 requirements or disciplinary record standards in order to maintain eligibility for institutional
299 scholarships and grants; provided, that academic achievement standards required to maintain
300 eligibility for federal student financial aid programs shall not be waived.
301 (b) The institution and its employees shall maintain confidentiality related to a waiver
302 and related personal identifying information unless disclosure is required specifically for the
303 purposes of executing the waiver.
304 Sec. 10. Data reporting requirements.
305 (a) No later than May 1 of each calendar year, institutions of higher education in the
306 District shall prepare and submit to the Higher Education Licensure Commission a report with
307 data from the past 12 months which includes:
308 (1) The number of allegations of sexual misconduct, broken down by allegations
309 of dating violence, domestic violence, sexual assault, and stalking, reported to the institution's
310 Title IX coordinator by a student or employee of the institution against another student or
311 employee of the institution;
13

312 (2) The number of law enforcement investigations initiated in response to
313 complaints of sexual misconduct brought forward by students and employees of the institution
314 against another student or employee of the institution, if known;
315 (3) The number of students and employees found responsible for violating an
316 institution's policies prohibiting sexual misconduct, and the number of students and employees
317 who were subjected to academic or employment disciplinary action;
318 (4) The number of students and employees investigated for sexual misconduct
319 who were found not responsible for violating an institution's sexual misconduct policies;
320 (5) The number of individual disciplinary actions imposed by the institution as a
321 result of a finding of responsibility for violating an institution's policies prohibiting sexual
322 misconduct;
323 (6) The number of students that requested accommodations and the number of
324 students for whom the institution granted accommodations; and
325 (7) The number of students that reported an incident of sexual misconduct and
326 took a leave of absence, transferred, or withdrew from the institution and identified in writing
327 when taking a leave of absence, transferring, or withdrawing that the incident of sexual
328 misconduct contributing to their decision.
329 (b) The report shall not contain personal identifying information. Demographic data
330 information contained in the report may be aggregated.
331 (c) Beginning in 2027, institutions in the District shall submit to the Higher Education
332 Licensure Commission and publish on the institution’s website the results of the online survey
333 for campus safety administered pursuant to section 1507 of the ‘Keeping Children Safe From
334 Family Violence Act,” approved March 15, 2022 (136 Stat. 959; 20 U.S.C. § 1161l-6).

335 Sec. 11. Enforcement.
336 (a) Upon determination, after reasonable notice and opportunity for a hearing, that an
337 institution of higher education has violated or failed to carry out any provision of this act or any
338 rule adopted under this act, the Higher Education Licensure Commission may impose a civil fine
339 upon an institution for each violation not to exceed $59,000, or one percent of an institution’s
340 annual operating budget, whichever is lower. The Higher Education Licensure Commission shall
341 use revenues generated from collecting civil penalty fines to execute any enforcement functions
342 required by this act.
