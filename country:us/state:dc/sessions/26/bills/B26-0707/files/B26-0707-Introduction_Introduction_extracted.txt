Statement of Introduction
Rodent Education and Management Yields Results (REMY) Amendment Act of 2026
June 22, 2026
Today, I am pleased to introduce the Rodent Education and Management Yields Results
(REMY) Amendment Act of 2026, along with Councilmembers Charles Allen, Matthew Frumin,
Janeese Lewis George, and Brooke Pinto. This legislation takes a public health approach to
preventing and abating growing rodent populations in the District. It focuses on addressing one of
the main causes of rodent infestations – significant amounts of accessible food – while also
providing public education on effective rodent abatement and protecting children, pets, and
wildlife from the health risks of rodenticides.
Discarded food and food scraps that are not properly stored are a major driver of rodent
infestations in the District, particularly in commercial areas with restaurants and other food
establishments producing a significant amount of waste. Although there are rodent proof
dumpsters available for commercial businesses, they are significantly more expensive than
standard dumpsters and many businesses cannot afford the upgrade, instead relying on ineffective
traps and rodenticide sprays while continuing to provide an easy food source for rodents.
Because rodent problems continue to increase in the District, residents are more frequently
turning to rodenticides purchased online or at hardware stores that, when applied improperly, are
not effective at reducing rodent populations. These rodenticides do, however, pose significant
health risks to residents, particularly children, as well as pets and endangered wildlife. One local
hospital reported 12 cases of pediatric hospitalization tied to rodenticide exposure in the past year.
Pets and wildlife are also susceptible to ingesting and being poisoned by rodenticides, particularly
owls, eagles, and other birds of prey.
The REMY Act proposes to both strategically decrease food sources for rodents,
while protecting residents from the health risks associated with untrained application of
rodenticides. Specifically, this bill proposes to:
• Decrease food sources for rodents by establishing a commercial corridor rodent-proof
dumpster pilot program to support Business Improvement Districts and Main Streets in
addressing areas with high rodent populations;
• Protect children, pets, and wildlife from rodenticide poisoning by requiring training and
certification for the sale and usage of anticoagulant rodenticides in the District; and
• Increase public awareness for effective prevention and abatement strategies by requiring
DC Health to conduct public education as part of its rodent abatement efforts.

23 To amend Subtitle B of the Rodent Control Act of 2000 to create a pilot program within the
24 Department of Health to provide grants to Business Improvement Districts and Main
25 Streets to subsidize or purchase rodent proof trash receptables for commercial businesses
26 in rodent hot spots, to limit the use of rodenticides by persons not trained or licensed as
27 pesticide applicators, and to require the Department of Health to conduct a public
28 education campaign on rodent prevention and control and rodenticide health risks.
29
30 BE IT ENACTED BY THE COUNCIL OF THE DISTRICT OF COLUMBIA, That this
31 act may be cited as the “Rodent Education and Management Yields Results Amendment Act of
32 2026”.
33 Sec. 2. Subtitle B of the Rodent Control Act of 2000, effective February 2, 2001 (D.C. 37
34 Law 13-172; D.C. Official Code § 8-2103.01 et seq.), is amended as follows:
35 (a) Section 904 (D.C. Official Code § 8-2103.01) is amended by added a new paragraph
36 (1A) and (1B) to read as follows:
37 “(1A) “Anticoagulant rodenticide” means a pesticide product intended to kill a rat
1

38 or mouse that has a mode of action that interferes with blood clotting, including products with
39 Brodifacoum, Bromadiolone, Chlorophacinone, Difenacoum, Difethialone, Diphacinonon, or
40 Warfarin as active ingredients.
41 “(1B) “Bureau” means the Bureau of Rodent Control with the Department of
42 Health.”.
43 (b) New sections 909, 910, and 911 are added to read as follows:
44 “Sec. 909. Establishment of commercial corridor rodent-proof dumpster pilot grant
45 program.
46 “(a) The Bureau shall establish a pilot grant program to provide grants to install at least
47 10 rodent-proof dumpsters for businesses in commercial corridors with high rat populations.
48 “(b) To be eligible to participate in the pilot program, an applicant shall:
49 “(1) Be a Business Improvement District, Main Street, or private food-serving
50 business;
51 “(2) Be located in a commercial corridor where the Bureau has identified there to
52 be a high incidence of rodents; and
53 “(3) Demonstrate that current dumpsters are causing increased rodents in a
54 commercial corridor and have consent from a business to replace one or more current dumpsters
55 with rodent-proof dumpsters.
56 “(c) The Bureau shall monitor and evaluate the pilot program for a period of no less than
57 12 months following installation of the rodent-proof dumpsters and assess factors including:
58 “(A) Reduction in rodent activity and complaints in pilot areas;
59 “(B) Business compliance with proper use of rodent-proof dumpsters, including
60 adequate frequency of waste collection; and
2

61 “(C) Durability, maintenance requirements, and user satisfaction of different
62 dumpster designs, if grantees purchase different models.
63 “(d) Within 18 months of the pilot program’s commencement, the Department of Health
64 shall submit a report to the Council detailing the program’s findings, recommendations for
65 expanding the pilot program, and proposed specifications for standardized rodent-proof
66 dumpsters.
67 “Sec. 910. Rodenticide use restrictions.
68 “(a) It shall be unlawful for a person to use, sell, or otherwise distribute for use
69 anticoagulant rodenticides on public or private property; provided, that this section shall not
70 prohibit the purchase or use of anticoagulant rodenticides by:
71 “(1) A District or federal government agency acting within the scope of its official
72 duties;
73 “(2) A contractor acting on behalf of a local or federal government agency; or
74 “(3) A company or person certified to apply pesticides by the Department of
75 Energy and the Environment, when acting within the scope of that certification.
76 “(b) A person who violates subsection (a) of this section shall remove the anticoagulant
77 rodenticide within 7 days of notification of the violation from the Mayor. A person who fails to
78 remove the rodenticide or who receives more than 2 notifications of violations shall be subject to
79 civil fines, penalties, and fees consistent with section 908(d).
80 “(c) Anticoagulant rodenticide shall be considered restricted-use pesticide pursuant to
81 D.C. Code § 8–432.
82 “Sec. 911. Public education on rodent abatement.
83 “(a) The Bureau shall establish a public education program (“program”) to reduce rodent
3

84 populations, which shall include information on the following topics:
85 “(1) Effective rodent prevention strategies, including sanitation, habitat
86 modification, and biological control;
87 “(2) Evidence-based rodent abatement strategies, including available government
88 and community resources;
89 “(3) Safe practices for encountering rodents to minimize risk of disease
90 transmission; and
91 “(4) Public health risks to children, adults, pets, and wildlife of improper use of
92 rodenticides, including emergency response procedures when a human or pet has ingested
93 rodenticide.
94 “(b) The program may:
95 “(1) Distribute printed materials, including brochures, mailers, or door hangers;
96 “(2) Create digital content, including website resources, social medial, and
97 interactive tools;
98 “(3) Conduct in-person trainings and community events;
99 “(4) Take out bus and metro banners;
100 “(5) Conduct partnerships with community organizations, schools, faith-based
101 institutions, and business associations; and
102 “(6) Conduct direct outreach to high-priority parties, including food service
103 establishments, property managers and owners, landlords, event venues, and other parties near
104 areas with high rodent activity.”.
