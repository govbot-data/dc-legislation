MURIEL BOWSER
MAYOR
October 10, 2025
The Honorable Phil Mendelson
Chairman
Council of the District of Columbia
John A. Wilson Building
1350 Pennsylvania Avenue, NW, Suite 504
Washington, DC 20004
Dear Chairman Mendelson:
Enclosed for consideration and adoption by the Council of the District of Columbia is a proposed
resolution titled “Early Childhood Academy Public Charter School Revenue Bonds Project Approval
Resolution of 2025”.
The resolution authorizes the issuance, sale, and delivery of tax-exempt revenue bonds, notes, or other
obligations in aggregate principal amount not to exceed $15,000,000. The bonds will be used to refinance
costs of the Early Childhood Academy Public Charter School for its school located at 885 Barnaby Street
SE, in Ward 8.
In accordance with section 490 of the Home Rule Act, it has been determined that the bonds, when, as,
and if issued, shall be without recourse to the District. The bonds shall not be general obligations of the
District; shall not be a pledge of or involve the full faith and credit or the taxing power of the District;
shall not constitute a debt of the District; and shall not constitute a lending of public credit for a private
undertaking as prohibited in section 602(a)(2) of the Home Rule Act. The bonds shall not give rise to any
pecuniary liability of the District, and the District shall have no obligation with respect to the purchase of
the bonds.
I urge the Council to take prompt and favorable action on the enclosed measure.
Sincerely,
Muriel Bowser

Early Childhood Academy Public Charter School
Revenue Bond
FACT SHEET
The Early Childhood Academy Public Charter School has requested that the District issue up to
$15 million in tax-exempt bonds for the refinancing of cost related to its location at 885 Barnaby
Street, SE, in Ward 8.
The Applicant
Founded in 2005, Early Childhood Academy Public Charter School (ECAPCS) serves
approximately 250 students in a nurturing and academically focused environment. ECAPCS is
dedicated to the academic and social development of each child, providing a safe, holistic learning
space where students become high achievers, proficient readers, and critical thinkers prepared to
thrive as lifelong, productive, and compassionate citizens.
ECAPCS is applying to the Industrial Revenue Bond (IRB) program to access affordable debt
financing as part of a longer-term plan regarding the financing of its initial facility acquisition
costs. Without the IRB program financing, the school would face higher interest rates and stricter
loan-to-value limitations typically imposed by traditional bank financing.
The Proposed Project
The proceeds of the bonds will be used to refinance ECAPCS’s costs associated with acquiring
and improving its property located at 885 Barnaby Street, SE, in Ward 8.
ECAPCS is scheduled to unwind its New Markets Tax Credit (NMTC) financing when the
proposed bonds close. The proposed refinancing using the IRB program is part of the school's
long-term financial strategy, as the NMTC financing originally used for the purchase and
development of the new facility will expire in September 2025. As part of this plan, ECAPCS
intends to refinance approximately $11,317,939 of existing loan principal through the issuance of
tax-exempt bonds, along with an estimated $2,000,000 in subordinated debt from the DC Office
of the State Superintendent of Education
Financing Plan
A summary of the proposed sources and uses of funds is attached (see Table 1).
Feasibility/Structure/Security of the Bonds
Equitable Facilities Fund, the underwriter for the proposed issuance, considers the proposed
revenue bond transaction to be financially feasible.
Early Childhood Academy Public Charter School

The bonds, if issued, will be secured by a general obligation pledge of ECAPCS and will be
without recourse to the District of Columbia.
Public Purpose Benefits
In 2019, Early Childhood Academy Public Charter School (ECAPCS) constructed a new
educational facility located at 885 Barnaby Street SE, Washington, DC. The three-story building
encompasses 37,686 square feet and includes fifteen (15) classrooms, six (6) administrative
offices, three (3) special education pullout spaces, a multipurpose room with food preparation
facilities, adult restrooms, and six (6) children’s restrooms. The site also features associated
improvements, including a playground and on-site parking.
The IRB program enables ECAPCS to borrow at lower, tax-exempt rates, freeing up valuable
resources that can be redirected toward its core mission of providing high-quality education to
children in the District of Columbia. Additionally, ECAPCS will be able to secure financing
without the need for the 25 to 30 percent equity contribution generally required by conventional
lenders. This increased access to capital is essential for supporting the school’s growth and
sustainability, enabling it to continue to serve the community effectively.
ECAPCS is deeply committed to expanding access to high-quality early childhood education in
the District of Columbia, addressing the critical developmental needs of young learners. By
providing an additional academic option for families, the school plays an important role in
providing educational choice and equality within the community. The proposed refinancing will
significantly enhance the financial sustainability of the school by reducing debt service costs. This,
in turn, will allow ECAPCS to redirect more resources toward essential educational services,
instructional materials, classroom equipment, and teacher support—ultimately strengthening the
learning environment for all students.
Legal and Regulatory Affairs
The law firm of Orrick, bond counsel to the Revenue Bond Program, has preliminarily determined
that the applicant is a 501(c)(3) organization and that this project constitutes a permissible
undertaking according to Section 490(a)(1) of the District of Columbia Home Rule Act.
Based on the information set forth in the application, the Revenue Bond Program staff has
determined that the proposed project complies with criteria for approval of this proposed financing
through the District’s Industrial Revenue Bond Program and will assist the borrower in furthering
its organizational mission.

Early Childhood Academy PCS
Annual Report Narrative
I. School Description
A. Mission Statement
Early Childhood Academy Public Charter School’s mission is to foster the academic and
social/emotional growth and development of each student in a safe and holistic learning environment
that will equip all students with the knowledge and tools to become high achievers, proficient readers,
and critical thinkers who will thrive for a lifetime as productive and caring citizens.
B. School Program
ECA serves children in PK3 through 3rd grade. Students must be three years old by September 30th
of the year of enrollment to be eligible for the PK3 program.
1. School Program - Summary of Curriculum Design and Instructional Approach
Early Childhood Academy PCS’s (ECA) primary focus during the 2023-2024 continued to be on
mitigating learning loss due to the pandemic. ECA's instructional program for prekindergarten-3
through grade 3 has a strong emphasis on language and literacy integrated with math, science, social
studies, and the arts.
Accelerated learning is designed to ensure reading proficiency, critical thinking, vocabulary
development, and mathematical thinking, ensuring that all ECA students are proficient readers and
mathematical thinkers by third grade. ECA provides instruction based on the Common Core State
Standards and bases all instruction for kindergarten through grade three on these standards. Through
the use of ongoing formative assessments, research-based curriculum materials, and data-informed
instruction, ECA's emphasis is on high quality teaching and learning for all students in all grades.
To support efforts to continue mitigation of learning loss, a full-time reading interventionist continues
to provide services to students within the general education population who were performing
significantly below grade level in reading.
Curriculum: ECA provides instruction using the Houghton Mifflin Harcourt (HMH) Into Reading, Into
Math, and Into Science programs for students in kindergarten through grade 3. The HMH Into Reading
curriculum is based on research in the essential elements of literacy, including the implementation of
structured literacy, as a part of the Science of Reading. With HMH Into Math, students build productive
perseverance in problem solving and apply knowledge to higher-level mathematics and beyond. HMH
Into Science utilizes the Next Generation Science Standards (NGSS) to provide coherent learning that
incorporates the claims, evidence, and reasoning model.

Students in prekindergarten are provided instruction using the Every Child Ready instructional model
published by AppleTree Institute. The Every Child Ready instructional model is rooted in seven guiding
principles. These guiding principles establish a foundation for development, implementation, and
decision making that values the knowledge, needs, and experiences of children, families, teachers,
and leaders.
Social Development: ECA’s instructional program is supported using Positive Behavior Facilitation
(PBF), which guides teachers’ responses to student behavior. ECA recognizes that academic
achievement is directly aligned with social/emotional well-being. Positive Behavior Facilitation is an
approach to student interaction that fosters safe, responsive, and nurturing classrooms and schools.
PBF is based on the beliefs that teachers must respond rather than react to student needs; that
teachers must recognize that their own belief systems affect their responses to students; and that
both teachers and students have “icebergs,” that which lies beneath the service but has a significant
impact on one’s behavior.
Additionally, ECA utilized the services of a full-time counselor provided by MBI Health, who provided
social/emotional and behavioral support to referred students.
English Language Arts: All children are exposed to a wide variety of books and stories that include an
abundance of literary and informational text. ECA’s English Language Arts program is implemented in
a two-hour morning block using the HMH Into Reading program. The block includes whole group
implementation of structured literacy, followed by daily teacher-led small group guided reading, and
instructional practice activities. All students are given ongoing reading assessments that identify their
independent reading levels – the levels at which they can read with deep understanding and fluency.
Students are expected to appropriately increase their reading levels over the course of the school
year, achieving “typical growth” as measured by the Measures of Academic progress assessment.
Information about reading levels will be given to families at each quarterly conference, but caregivers
are always welcome to ask their child’s teacher for specifics about their children’s levels and progress.
Mathematics: Students participate in whole group and small group math activities for one hour daily
using the HMH Into Math program. Math instruction includes developing students’ conceptual
understanding and procedural skills, encouraging perseverance and practice in grappling with
problems, and integrating technology to support learning.
Science: Following the Next Generation Science Standards, ECA students in all grade levels study life
sciences, earth and space sciences, physical sciences, and engineering, technology, and application
of sciences through implementation of the HMH Into Science curriculum.
Social Studies: Focus is on what it means to become a good citizen, extending from the home, to the
neighborhood, to the classroom, to the community, and beyond. Students begin to understand what
it means to be a contributing member of society.

Health and Fitness: Health promotion and wellness at ECA is based on DC standards and in accordance
with the Healthy Schools Act. A full-time health and physical education teacher provides weekly
classes for all grade levels with targeted focused on building physical fitness and a healthy lifestyle.
Music: ECA provides a robust music program that includes weekly general music instruction for all
ECA classes, as well as instrumental music for identified students in kindergarten through grade three.
All students are exposed to a wide variety of music genres, introduced to the different instruments
and their groups, and taught rhythm and beat. Students receiving instrumental music instruction learn
to play the violin, viola, cello, xylophone, and drums, and participate in our string and percussion
orchestras.
Art: Students participate in weekly art classes that expose them to a variety of media, including
drawing, painting, sculpting, and crafting. All students
Inclusion Program: ECA celebrates the diversity of its students and believes that all students, including
those with disabilities, can achieve at high levels. We believe that all students benefit from the inclusion
of students with disabilities in the general education classroom. Both research and our own experience
demonstrate that students with disabilities perform better with greater access to the general
education curriculum and non-disabled peers. Our special education program is designed to provide
access to our students with disabilities through Individualized Education Programs (IEPs).
The inclusion program and team have two main priorities: to
• identify students with disabilities in accordance with IDEA, through an appropriate and timely
referral, evaluation, and eligibility process that includes the use of the RTI process before
referral; and to
• provide effective support to students with disabilities so that they may meet their IEP goals.
At ECA, students with exceptionalities receive many of their services within the regular education
setting through the collaborative efforts of therapists, push in special education teachers, and general
education teachers. General education teachers and special educators share teaching responsibilities
in the general education classroom in a variety of ways: one teaching/one supporting, leading small
groups, parallel teaching, alternative teaching, and team teaching. In addition, both teachers provide
environmental and lesson-specific modifications and accommodations to support students’ success
throughout the day.
Based on the student’s IEP goals, therapists and special education teachers work with students
individually or in small groups within the general education classroom for push in services or outside
of the classroom in a resource room.

The Special Education Coordinator manages the evaluation, eligibility and IEP process and meets with
special education teachers, general education teachers, social workers, and related service providers
to coordinate and schedule Multidisciplinary Team (MDT) Meetings and assessments, monitor the
success of the push-in and pull-out programs, and to assess school-wide professional development
needs pertaining to students with disabilities.
ECA has implemented the Response to Intervention Model (RTI) and Positive Behavior Facilitation
strategies to support maintenance of an emotionally healthy environment for children where they can
reach their fullest potential. Part of our expectation for teachers is to provide positive feedback to
students, both individually and as a group, to highlight and celebrate on-task behavior. Each teacher
has developed a strategy for noticing students following the school rules and often children receive
specific praise for their positive behavior. Teachers also issue Class Dojo points to incentivize students’
good citizenship.
Professional Development: ECA’s core beliefs are that learning is a lifelong endeavor and that students
succeed when teachers, administrators, and school staff are continually learning and growing
themselves. In the 2023-2024 school year, ECA provided high quality, ongoing professional
development to all staff members and continued a school culture of adult learning. ECA provided a
two-week period of teacher training in August, monthly training through March, as well as weekly
collaborative planning meetings for all teachers. During the school year, key staff persons received
external training through OSSE, HMH Publishers, the Special Education Cooperative, and AppleTree
Institute for Education Innovation.
2. School Program – Parent Involvement
ECA continues to prioritize building strong relationships and a sense of community among our students,
their families, and our faculty and staff. The minimum participation expected of all parents is
attendance at the parent-teacher conferences held quarterly and scheduled individually. During the
2023-2024 school year, parent meetings were held in person.
ECA believes that all stakeholders bring valuable contributions. The Parent Engagement Committee
(PEC) provides all parents with a venue in which to meet, share concerns and recommendations, and
learn more about the Schoolwide Plan. The PEC meets every other quarter and is open to all parents.
Back to School Night for all parents was held September 20, 2023. Teachers introduced themselves
and outlined the objectives and skills students would be learning during the school year.
Individual Parent-Teacher Conferences were held November 3, 2023 and February 16, 2024. Parent-
Teacher conferences offered parents the opportunity to meet individually with teachers to discuss their
child’s academic growth and development and receive report cards.

II. School Performance
A. Performance and Progress
1. THE EXTENT TO WHICH THE SCHOOL IS MEETING ITS MISSION, DETAILING
PROGRAMS AND/OR METHODOLOGIES THROUGH WHICH THE SCHOOL PURSUES ITS
MISSION.
During the 2023-2024 school year, Early Childhood Academy Public Charter School continued to work
toward mitigating the learning loss that many students continued to demonstrate after the pandemic
and virtual learning. Through the use of the Every Child Ready instructional program and the
Classroom Assessment Scoring System (CLASS) for prekindergarten, the research based HMH reading
and math for kgn – grade 3, and the shift to the Science of Reading Structured Literacy approach,
students were provided a rigorous, standards-based reading and math program.
Tier 2 support for struggling learners is implemented through the Response to Intervention model,
providing identified students with an additional 15-20 minutes of individualized or small group
intervention in ELA and/or math in the classroom setting. Three full-time special education teachers
and five special education associate teachers, in addition to contracted service providers for speech
services, OT, PT, and counseling, served our 52 students with disabilities in a push-in, pull-out, or
full-time model, based on the student’s IEP. Finally, our use of Positive Behavior Facilitation school-
wide ensures that responses to students’ behavior needs are nurturing, supportive, and
developmentally appropriate.
ECA continued to ensure students' one to one access to technology, providing a Chromebook for each
kindergarten through third grade student and an iPad for each prekindergarten student’s classroom
use.

B. Unique Accomplishments
ECA underwent reaccreditation during the spring of 2022. On November 4, 2022, ECA was notified
that the Commissions on Elementary and Secondary Schools of the Middle States Association of
Colleges and Schools voted to award Early Childhood Academy Public Charter School seven-year
accreditation. Accreditation will expire January 1, 2030.
The Joyful Market sponsored by Martha’s Table provided free fresh fruits, vegetables, and protein
items to all ECA parents through monthly drop offs.
ECA continues to maintain a partnership with MBI Health Services, who provide in-school,
individualized and group counseling for students referred because of social/emotional or behavioral
challenges.
ECA partnered with the Department of Health and Gordon Dental Associates to provide free in-house
preventative dental screenings for all ECA students.
Our Girls on the Run team, which includes 3rd grade girls, exposes students to social, emotional,
physical, and behavioral skills to successfully navigate life experiences. All participating girls received
a running shoe scholarship. The program’s intentional curriculum places an emphasis on developing
competence, confidence, connection, character, caring, and contribution in young girls through lessons
that incorporate running and other physical activities.
ECA invested in social media outreach. Over 1,050 parents, families and organizations interacted with
the school via Facebook, Twitter, and Instagram social media platforms.
During ECA’s Family Fun Night event, students were gifted free books from The Bookshelf Project and
backpacks from Black Child Development.
In support of Early Childhood Academy PCS basketball and cheerleading teams, ECA parents, teachers,
staff, and board members raised $4,600.00 in the Fall Double Good Popcorn Fundraiser toward the
purchase of team uniforms.
Additionally, every year during the winter holiday season ECA hosts an ECA Angels Toy Drive. Gifts are
donated and distributed to children from their assigned ECA Angel (staff member) based on identified
needs of the families.
