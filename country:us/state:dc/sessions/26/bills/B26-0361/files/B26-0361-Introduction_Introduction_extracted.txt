Statement of Introduction
The Shine Safely Act of 2025
September 22, 2025
Today, alongside Councilmembers Pinto, Allen, and Nadeau, I am introducing the Shine Safely
Act of 2025, legislation that will phase out mercury-containing compact fluorescent and linear
fluorescent lamps in the District of Columbia to protect public health, the environment, and
workers.
While fluorescent lighting has been common for decades, LED alternatives are now widely
available, use roughly half as much electricity, and cost less to operate over time. The slightly
higher upfront price of LEDs continues to decline each year and is more than offset by lower
electric bills, making them a practical choice for residents and businesses alike. Transitioning to
LEDs also reduces planet-warming emissions from power plants and lowers demand on the
electric grid.
Fluorescent lighting poses significant risks when it breaks and releases mercury. Even small
amounts of mercury exposure can damage the nervous, immune, and renal systems and harm
lung function. Children and fetuses are especially vulnerable, with mercury exposure potentially
impairing cognitive, language, motor, and visual development and affecting human reproduction.
Workers who handle the removal, disposal, or recycling of fluorescent lamps, as well as
communities near disposal sites, face heightened risks.
The World Health Organization lists mercury among the top ten chemicals of major public health
concern, and ten U.S. states have already enacted laws restricting the sale and distribution of
mercury-containing fluorescent lighting. Compared to these jurisdictions, the District’s standards
remain outdated.
The Shine Safely Act of 2025 provides a practical, commonsense fix. By phasing out the sale
and distribution of mercury-containing fluorescent lighting, it advances public health,
environmental protection, worker safety, and long-term cost savings for District residents and
businesses. I look forward to working with my colleagues to move this legislation forward and
help ensure a safer, more energy-efficient future for our community.

27 Sec. 2. Definitions.
28 For purposes of this act, the term:
29 (1) “Compact fluorescent lamp” means a compact low-pressure, mercury-containing,
30 electric discharge light source in which a fluorescent coating transforms some of the ultraviolet energy
31 generated by the mercury discharge into visible light, and includes all of the following characteristics:
32 (A) One base (end cap) of any type, including screw, bayonet, 2 pins, and 4
33 pins;
34 (B) Integrally ballasted or non-integrally ballasted;

35 (C) Light emission between a correlated color temperature of 1700K and
36 24000K and a Duv of +0.024 and -0.024 in the International Commission on Illumination (“CIE”)
37 Uniform Color Space (“CAM02-UCS”);
38 (D) All tube diameters and all tube lengths; and
39 (E) All lamp sizes and shapes for directional and nondirectional installations,
40 including: PL, spiral, twin tube, triple twin, 2D, U-bend, and circular.
41 (2) “Linear fluorescent lamp” means a low-pressure, mercury-containing, electric-
42 discharge light source in which fluorescent coating transforms some of the ultraviolet energy
43 generated by the mercury discharge into visible light, and includes all of the following characteristics:
44 (A) Two bases (end caps) of any type, including single-pin, 2-pin, and recessed
45 double contact;
46 (B) Light emission between a correlated color temperature of 1700K and
47 24000K and a Duv of +0.024 and -0.024 in the CIE CAM02-UCS;
48 (C) All tube diameters, including, but not limited to, T5, T8, T10, and T12;
49 (D) All tube lengths from 0.5 to 8.0 feet, inclusive; and
50 (E) All lamp shapes, including linear, U-bend, and circular.
51 Sec. 3. Phasing out.
52 (a) On and after July 1, 2027, it shall be a violation of this act to offer for final sale, sell at final
53 sale, or distribute in the District as a new manufactured product a screw or bayonet base type compact
54 fluorescent lamp.
55 (b) On and after July 1, 2028, it shall be a violation of this act to offer for final sale, sell at
56 final sale, or distribute in the District as a new manufactured product a pin-base type compact
57 fluorescent lamp or a linear fluorescent lamp.

58 (c) This act shall not apply to a lamp:
59 (1) Designed and marketed exclusively for image capture and projection, including:
60 (A) Photocopying;
61 (B) Printing, directly or in preprocesses;
62 (C) Lithography;
63 (D) Film or video projection; or
64 (E) Holography.
65 (2) Has a high proportion of ultraviolet light emission and:
66 (A) With high ultraviolet content that has ultraviolet power greater than two
67 milliwatts per kilolumen (mW/klm);
68 (B) For germicidal use, such as the destruction of DNA, that emits a peak
69 radiation of approximately 253.7 nanometers;
70 (C) Designed and marketed exclusively for disinfection or fly trapping from
71 which either the radiation power emitted between 250 and 315 nanometers represents at least 5
72 percent of, or the radiation power emitted between 315 and 400 nanometers represents at least 20

74 (D) Designed and marketed exclusively for the generation of ozone, where the
75 primary purpose is to emit radiation at approximately 185.1 nanometers;
76 (E) Designed and marketed exclusively for coral zooxanthellae symbiosis from

79 (F) Designed and marketed exclusively for use in a sunlamp product, as that
80 term is defined in 21 C.F.R. § 1040.20(b)(9) in effect on the effective date of act;

93 Sec. 4. Implementation.
94 (a)(1) The Department of Energy and the Environment (“DOEE”) shall conduct periodic
95 inspections of distributors or retailers in order to determine compliance with the provisions of this
96 act.
97 (2) DOEE shall track all complaints received concerning violations of this act, the
98 agency’s response to each complaint, and the resolution of each complaint.
99 (b)(1) If DOEE finds that a person has committed a violation of any provision of this act,
100 DOEE shall issue a Notice of Violation to such person if it is their first violation.
101 (2) A person who commits a second violation shall be subject to a civil penalty of up
102 to $100 for each offense.
4

103 (3) A person who commits any further violations shall be subject to a civil penalty of
104 not more than $500 for each offense.
105 (c) If a person commits a fifth violation of the provisions of this act, it shall be referred to
106 the Office of the Attorney General, who may institute proceedings in Superior Court to enforce the
107 provisions of this act.
108 (d) The Mayor, pursuant to Title I of the District of Columbia Administrative Procedure
109 Act, approved October 21, 1968 (82 Stat. 1204; D.C. Official Code § 2-501 et seq.), may issue rules
110 to implement the provisions of this act.
