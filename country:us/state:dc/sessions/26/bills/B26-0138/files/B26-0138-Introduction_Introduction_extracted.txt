Dear Secretary Howard:
Today, I am introducing the “Fair Swipe Act of 2025”, along with Councilmembers Anita Bonds,
Wendell Felder, Matthew Frumin, Christina Henderson, Janeese Lewis George, Brianne K.
Nadeau, Brooke Pinto, and Robert C. White, Jr. Please find enclosed a signed copy of the
legislation.
Restaurants and eateries, large and small, and other local business retailers are the cornerstones
of our communities and an engine of our economy. According to the National Restaurant
Association, in 2024, the restaurant industry exceeded $1.1 trillion in sales and remitted $216.7
billion in taxes for all levels of government. However, providing this tax collection service for
jurisdictions comes at a high cost for business owners because when a consumer decides to pay
their bill with either their debit or credit card, the debit or credit card network charges an
interchange fee (also known as a “swipe fee”) for the transaction. A swipe fee, which averages 2-
4% per transaction, is also charged on the tax and tips portion of the bill, even though the
business never pockets that money and passes the taxes and tips portion on to the jurisdiction
or employee. A swipe fee on the sales and tax portions of a bill unfairly eats into the hard-earned
profits of our local business retailers and should stay in their pockets so that they can continue
to grow their businesses.
This bill would prohibit the charging of interchange fees on the sales tax and gratuity portions of
credit and debit card transactions. It also outlines the process by which a merchant can submit
tax and gratuity documentation and receive credits for the amount of interchange fees charged
per electronic payment transaction. For violations of the prohibitions created in this bill, it would
impose a civil penalty of $1,000 per electronic payment transaction. Finally, the bill includes a
non-severability clause, thereby ensuring key provisions do not solely burden District- chartered
banks should the legislation be challenged in court.
1

This legislation would allow the District to ensure that all merchants can keep their hard-earned
profits in their pockets by recovering these fees either at the point of sale or through retroactive
reimbursement by a financial institution. This is a step toward ensuring our local businesses
continue to have a fair shot at post-pandemic recovery and can thrive in an environment that
recognizes their challenges and creates opportunities for owners/operators and workers to
succeed.
Sincerely,
Councilmember Charles Allen, Ward 6
Chairperson, Committee on Transportation & the Environment

32 To prohibit the charging of interchange fees on the sales tax and gratuity portions of credit and
33 debit card transactions, to outline the process by which a merchant can submit tax and
34 gratuity documentation and receive a credit for the amount of interchange fees charged
35 per electronic payment transaction, to impose a civil penalty of $1,000 per electronic
36 payment transaction for violations of this act, and to provide that this act is non-
37 severable.
38
39 BE IT ENACTED BY THE COUNCIL OF THE DISTRICT OF COLUMBIA, That this
40 act may be cited as the “Fair Swipe Act of 2025”.
41 Sec. 2. Definitions.
42 For the purposes of this act, the term:
1

43 (1) "Acquirer bank" means a member of a payment card network that contracts
44 with a merchant for the settlement of electronic payment transactions. An acquirer bank may
45 contract directly with merchants or indirectly through a processor to process electronic payment
46 transactions.
47 (2) "Authorization" means the process through which a merchant requests
48 approval for an electronic payment transaction from the issuer.
49 (3) "Clearance" means the process of transmitting final transaction data from a
50 merchant to an issuer for posting to the cardholder's account and the calculation of fees and
51 charges, including interchange fees, that apply to the issuer and the merchant.
52 (4) "Credit card" means a card, plate, coupon book, or other credit device existing
53 for the purpose of obtaining money, property, labor, or services on credit.
54 (5) "Debit card" means a card or other payment code or device issued or approved
55 for use through a payment card network to debit an asset account, regardless of the purpose for
56 which the account is established, whether authorization is based on a signature, a personal
57 identification number, or other means. The term includes a general use prepaid card, as defined
58 in 15 U.S.C. § 1693l-1, but does not include paper checks.
59 (6) "Electronic payment transaction" means a transaction in which a person uses a
60 debit card, a credit card, or other payment code or device issued or approved through a payment
61 card network to debit a deposit account or use a line of credit, whether authorization is based on
62 a signature, a personal identification number, or other means.
63 (7) "Gratuity" means a voluntary monetary contribution to an employee from a
64 guest, patron, or customer in connection with services rendered.
2

65 (8) "Interchange fee" means a fee established, charged, or received by a payment
66 card network for the purpose of compensating the issuer for its involvement in an electronic
67 payment transaction.
68 (9) "Issuer" means a person issuing a debit card or credit card or the issuer's agent.
69 (10) "Merchant" means a person that accepts electronic payment transactions and
70 collects and remits a tax.
71 (11) "Payment card network" means an entity that:
72 (A) Directly or through licensed members, processors, or agents, provides
73 the proprietary services, infrastructure, and software to route information and data for the
74 purpose of conducting electronic payment transaction authorization, clearance, and settlement;
75 and
76 (B) A merchant uses to accept as a form of payment a brand of debit card,
77 credit card, or other device that may be used to carry out electronic payment transactions.
78 (12) "Person" means any individual, firm, public or private corporation,
79 government, partnership, association, or any other organization or entity.
80 (13) "Processor" means an entity that facilitates, services, processes, or manages
81 the debit or credit authorization, billing, transfer, payment procedures, or settlement with respect
82 to any electronic payment transaction.
83 (14) "Settlement" means the process of transmitting sales information to the
84 issuing bank for collection and reimbursement of funds to the merchant and calculating and
85 reporting the net transaction amount to the issuer and merchant for an electronic payment
86 transaction that is cleared.
87 (15) "Tax" means sales and use tax imposed pursuant to D.C. Official Code § 47-
88 2002.
3

89 (16) "Tax and gratuity documentation" means documentation or data sufficient for
90 the payment card network to determine the total amount of the electronic payment transaction
91 and the tax or gratuity amount of the transaction. Tax documentation may be related to a single
92 electronic payment transaction or multiple electronic payment transactions aggregated over a
93 period of time. Examples of tax documentation include invoices, receipts, journals, ledgers, and
94 tax returns filed with the Office of Tax and Revenue.
95 Sec. 3. Prohibition on charging of interchange fees on sales tax part of credit and debit
96 card transactions.
97 (a) An issuer, a payment card network, an acquirer bank, or a processor shall not receive
98 or charge a merchant any interchange fee on the tax amount or gratuity of an electronic payment
99 transaction if the merchant informs the acquirer bank or its designee of the tax or gratuity amount
100 as part of the authorization or settlement process for the electronic payment transaction. The
101 merchant shall transmit the tax or gratuity amount data as part of the authorization or settlement
102 process to avoid being charged interchange fees on the tax or gratuity amount of an electronic
103 payment transaction.
104 (b) A merchant that does not transmit the tax or gratuity amount data in accordance with
105 this section as part of the authorization or settlement process may submit tax and gratuity
106 documentation for the electronic payment transaction to the acquirer bank or its designee no later
107 than 180 days after the date of the electronic payment transaction, and, within 30 days after the
108 merchant submits the necessary tax and gratuity documentation, the issuer or payment card
109 network shall credit to the merchant the amount of interchange fees charged on the tax or
110 gratuity amount of the electronic payment transaction.
111 (c) This section shall not be construed as imposing liability on a payment card network
112 for the accuracy of the tax or gratuity data reported by the merchant.
4

113 (d) It shall be unlawful for an issuer, a payment card network, an acquirer bank, or a
114 processor to alter or manipulate the computation and imposition of interchange fees by
115 increasing the rate or amount of the fees applicable to or imposed upon the portion of a credit or
116 debit card transaction not attributable to taxes or gratuities to circumvent the effect of this
117 section.
118 (e) An issuer, a payment card network, an acquirer bank, a processor, or other designated
119 entity that has received the tax or gratuity documentation and violates subsection (a) of this
120 section shall be subject to a civil penalty of $1,000 per electronic payment transaction and shall
121 refund the merchant the interchange fee calculated on the tax or gratuity amount relative to the
122 electronic payment transaction.
123 Sec. 4. Non-severability.
124 If any provision of section 3 of this act or its application to any person or circumstance is
125 held to be unconstitutional, beyond the statutory authority of the Council, or otherwise invalid,
126 then all provisions of this act shall be deemed invalid.
