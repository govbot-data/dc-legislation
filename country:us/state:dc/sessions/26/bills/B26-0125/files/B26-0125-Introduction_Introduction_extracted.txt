Dear Secretary Howard:
Today, I am introducing the “2607 Connecticut Avenue NW Timeline Extension and Tax
Forgiveness Act of 2025” along with Councilmember Robert C. White, Jr. This legislation, drafted
in consultation with the Office of Chief Financial Officer (OCFO), would allow the organization So
Others Might Eat (SOME) to apply for tax exemption pursuant to the Nonprofit Workforce Housing
Properties Real Property Tax Exemption Amendment Act of 2019. Subtitle M of D.C. Law 23-16,
the Fiscal Year 2020 Budget Support Act of 2019, 66 DCR 12631, effective Sept. 11, 2019.
The Council created this tax exemption to encourage private sector development of workforce
housing. Under its provisions, rental housing that is owned by a nonprofit entity and in which the
majority of tenants earn less—and no tenants earn much more—than the adjusted median income
and rents are kept at reasonable rates are exempted from certain real property taxes, allowing these
nonprofits to focus their financial resources on their good works.
For over 50 years, SOME has been a cornerstone of support for Washingtonians experiencing
poverty, homelessness, hunger, and related challenges. Through its comprehensive Whole Person
Care approach, SOME addresses five key areas: Housing, Emergency and Social Services,
Healthcare, Education and Workforce Development, and Advocacy. A dedicated housing provider
since the 1980s, SOME strengthened its commitment to affordable housing by launching the
Affordable Housing Development Initiative in 2005. SOME now operates over 1,300 housing
units across the city. SOME also provides its tenants with a comprehensive range of essential
services, including case management, to ensure they have the support needed to thrive and
contribute meaningfully to their community.
In 2023, SOME opened its first Ward 3 housing at 2607 Connecticut Avenue NW, providing 23
units of affordable housing in a Metro-accessible location. Initially, lease-up was delayed due to
the timing of the Department of Housing and Community Development’s (DHCD) consolidated

Request for Proposals (RFP) and SOME's ability to apply for funding. In addition, SOME
partnered with the Department of Human Services (DHS) to identify prospective tenants with
Permanent Supportive Housing (PSH) vouchers. However, the process faced challenges. Due to
these circumstances, only one lease was signed in the first year. However, SOME has built a strong
partnership with Woodley House, which provides referrals and supportive services to residents.
As a result, more than half of the units are now leased, with the remainder expected to be occupied
soon. Despite this progress, the intended incentive the Council intended to support SOME’s efforts
has been frustrated. If this situation is not addressed, SOME will be required to put money into
taxes that the Council wants it to be putting towards social services and the provision of additional
housing.
This legislation would extend the 12-month period to 36-months, allowing OCFO to accept and
process SOME’s application now and, if and when and for as long as SOME demonstrates that its
housing qualifies, to provide the exemption the Council intended such housing to have.
Should you have any questions about this legislation, please contact my Legislative Director, Conrad
Risher, at crisher@dccouncil.gov.
Sincerely,
Councilmember Matthew Frumin, Ward 3
Chairperson, Committee on Human Services
