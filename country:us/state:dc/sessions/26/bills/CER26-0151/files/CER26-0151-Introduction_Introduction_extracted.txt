26 A CEREMONIAL RESOLUTION
27 __________
28
29 IN THE COUNCIL OF THE DISTRICT OF COLUMBIA
30 __________
31
32
33 To officially declare May 1st, 2026, as D.C. Statehood Day in the District of Columbia.
34
35 WHEREAS, District of Columbia residents contribute more in total internal revenue than 30
36 states and more in business income taxes than 27 states;
37
38 WHEREAS, the over 700,000 residents of the District represent a diverse population with 42%
39 identifying as White Non-Hispanic, 24% as Black, 18% as Hispanic, 11% as Asian, and 5% as other;
40
41 WHEREAS, the dollar amount of federal taxes paid by the residents of the District of Columbia
42 in the past year currently stands at $2,065,143,245;
43
44 WHEREAS, over 1 million tourist travel to the District of Columbia annually to see the cherry
45 blossoms, and the District has 26 million visitors each year;
46
47 WHEREAS, the District has the number one rated park system in the United States with more
48 than 21% of the land used for parks and outdoor recreation, well above the national mean of 15%;
49

50 WHEREAS, the United States Constitution as ratified in 1788 provided the right to vote for
51 representation in Congress to the “People of the several States” of the United States, which included
52 citizens living on the land that would later be designated by the federal government for the nation’s
53 capital as ceded by Maryland and Virginia to become the District of Columbia;
54
55 WHEREAS, despite the provision of the right of vote in the Constitution, through the enactment
56 of the “District of Columbia Organic Act of 1801”, D.C. residents were disenfranchised from voting for
57 representative Members of Congress more than 200 years ago;
58
59 WHEREAS, the District of Columbia has more residents than the States of Vermont and
60 Wyoming, and almost as many as North Dakota;
61
62 WHEREAS, the residents of the District of Columbia continue to express their determination to
63 achieve full representation by electing two United States Senators and a United States Representative to
64 advocate for statehood and full democracy;
65
66 WHEREAS, citizens of the District of Columbia bear all of the responsibilities of citizenship,
67 including fighting in wars and serving in the military, paying federal income taxes, and serving on federal
68 juries;
69
70 WHEREAS, no other democratic nation denies basic rights of self-government, including
71 participation in the national legislature, to residents of its capital;
72
73 WHEREAS, the United Nations Human Rights Committee has released statements expressing
74 concern over this denial of representation to citizens in D.C.;
75
76 WHEREAS, the Organization of American States (OAS), to which the United States is a
77 signatory, has described the disenfranchisement of D.C. residents as a violation of its charter agreement;
78
79 WHEREAS, the residents of the District have no voting representation in Congress and therefore
80 cannot hold representatives accountable for arbitrary budget interference;
81
82 WHEREAS, despite nearly three decades of balanced budgets, D.C. faces the possibility each
83 year of being shut down because of congressional interference;
84
85 WHEREAS, it has been more than 50 years since the passage of the Civil Rights Act, and more
86 than 40 years since the District of Columbia was granted home rule, and the residents of the District of
87 Columbia have yet to have the same rights as the residents of the 50 states.
88
89 RESOLVED, BY THE COUNCIL OF THE DISTRICT OF COLUMBIA, that this resolution
90 may be cited as the “D.C. Statehood Day Recognition Resolution of 2026.”
91
92 Sec. 2. The Council of the District of Columbia hereby declares May 1st, 2026 to be D.C.
93 Statehood Day and encourages leaders and residents to discuss statehood for the District of Columbia in
94 high-profile settings in an effort to educate Americans regarding the lack of congressional voting
95 representation and budget autonomy for the residents of the District.
96
97 Sec. 3. This resolution shall take effect immediately upon the first date of publication in the
98 District of Columbia Register.
