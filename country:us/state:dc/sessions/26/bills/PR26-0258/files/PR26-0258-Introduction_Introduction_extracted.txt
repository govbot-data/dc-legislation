19 To declare an emergency with respect to the need to amend the Residential Tranquility Act of
20 2010 to prohibit persons targeting a residence for purposes of demonstration from using
21 sound amplifying devices in a residential zone between 7:00 p.m. and 9:00 a.m., to
22 prohibit a person from launching or throwing objects onto the residential property of
23 another with the intent to cause fear, intimidate, or harass any person living or working at
24 that residence, and to prohibit the use of unattended sound amplifying devices emitting
25 sound while targeting a residence; and to amend the National Capital Revitalization and
26 Self-Government Improvement Act of 1997 to provide that the term expiration date for a
27 Council appointment to the Corrections Information Council shall be December 7th of
28 the year in which the term expires.

32 RESOLVED, BY THE COUNCIL OF THE DISTRICT OF COLUMBIA, That this
33 resolution may be cited as the “Residential Tranquility Emergency Declaration Resolution of
34 2025”.
35 Sec. 2. (a) The District of Columbia have observed an alarming increase in targeted
36 demonstrations using amplified sound devices, creating significant disruptions that extend far
37 beyond any single residence to impact entire communities. These demonstrations, particularly
38 when employing amplified sound devices, fundamentally disturb the peace and tranquility that
1

39 residents should rightfully expect in their homes. As the Supreme Court emphasized in Carey v.
40 Brown, “The State's interest in protecting the well-being, tranquility, and privacy of the home is
41 certainly of the highest order in a free and civilized society.” Carey v. Brown, 447 U.S. 455, 471.
42 The Court has consistently recognized the home as “the last citadel of the tired, the weary, and
43 the sick,” Gregory v. Chicago, 394 U.S. 111, 125 (1969) (Black, J., concurring). and maintained
44 that “preserving the sanctity of the home, the one retreat to which men and women can repair to
45 escape from the tribulations of their daily pursuits, is surely an important value.” Carey, supra, at
46 471. One “important aspect of residential privacy” includes the “protection of the unwilling
47 listener” at home, especially when a particular residence is being targeted. Frisby v. Schultz, 487
48 U.S. 474, 484–85 (1988) (“a special benefit of the privacy all citizens enjoy within their own
49 walls, which the State may legislate to protect, is an ability to avoid intrusions. Thus, we have
50 repeatedly held that individuals are not required to welcome unwanted speech into their own
51 homes and that the government may protect this freedom”).
52 (b) The persistent use of amplified sound devices in residential areas poses significant
53 health risks to residents. Medical research has established that constant exposure to elevated
54 sound levels can trigger anxiety, elevated stress hormones, and adverse psychological effects.
55 These impacts are particularly severe for children whose cognitive development can be impaired
56 by chronic excessive sound exposure, and for elderly residents or residents with disabilities who
57 may be more sensitive to auditory disruptions. Current regulations have proven inadequate in
58 addressing the unique harm caused by targeted amplified sound at residences, leaving
59 communities without sufficient protections.
60 (c) The District's current law, which restricts certain activities between 10:00 p.m. and
61 7:00 a.m., does not align with the realities of modern life and work patterns. Many District
62 residents, including early morning commuters and night shift workers, require rest during hours
2

63 outside this window. Furthermore, families with school-age children, who typically require
64 between 9 and 11 hours of sleep, often begin their bedtime routines well before 10:00 p.m.
65 Further, the morning hours between 6:00 a.m. and 9:00 a.m. are particularly crucial for families
66 preparing for school and work, a time when quiet and focus are essential. Madsen v. Women's
67 Health Ctr., Inc., 512 U.S. 753, 772 (1994) (“We must, of course, take account of the place to
68 which the regulations apply in determining whether these restrictions burden more speech than
69 necessary. We have upheld similar noise restrictions in the past, and as we noted in upholding a
70 local noise ordinance around public schools”). Medical research has shown that inadequate sleep
71 resulting from noise disruption increases risks of cardiovascular disease, compromises immune
72 system function, and impairs cognitive performance. Demonstrations targeted at a residence can
73 cause these disruptions; the limitations imposed through this emergency legislation will help
74 address these unique harms.
75 (d) The District is also facing the emerging issue of unattended sound amplifying devices
76 being placed near residences. These devices, when left without active supervision, create unique
77 enforcement challenges and can subject residents to prolonged harassment with no accountable
78 party present to address concerns.
79 (e) The District has also observed a disturbing trend of individuals throwing objects at
80 residential properties as a means of harassment and intimidation. This behavior not only poses
81 immediate safety risks and potential property damage but also creates significant psychological
82 distress for residents who feel targeted and unsafe in their own homes. The current legal
83 framework does not adequately address this specific form of harassment, leaving a critical gap in
84 protections for District residents facing such intimidation tactics.
85 (f) On June 17, 2025 the Council approved first reading of B26-0189, the Residential
86 Tranquility Amendment Act of 2025, but the legislative process will take further time to become
3

87 law. Emergency legislation is necessary to provide protection for residential tranquility and the
88 safety of those who reside in the District. Current enforcement mechanisms have proven
89 insufficient to address these forms of harassment, leaving residents vulnerable to immediate and
90 ongoing harm. These changes represent an urgent and necessary approach to safeguarding the
91 well-being of District residents and addressing challenges to residential peace and security while
92 respecting established legal principles. The emergency nature of this legislation is justified by the
93 immediate need to protect residents from escalating forms of harassment and to ensure their
94 fundamental right to peace and security in their homes.
95 (g) This legislation is guided by the precedence and jurisprudence of the Supreme Court
96 surrounding sound amplification and the First Amendment, according to which “excessive”
97 noise and disturbance are substantial government interests that can justify narrowly tailored
98 regulations of constitutionally protected speech by time, place, and manner, and according to
99 which focused picketing solely in front of a particular residence may be proscribed. Generally
100 speaking, case law shows that courts are inclined to find ordinances prohibiting demonstrations
101 targeting particular residences as constitutional but not general ordinances banning picketing on
102 an entire residential street. Cf. Frisby v. Schultz, 487 U.S. 474 (1988); Veneklase v. City of
103 Fargo, 248 F.3d 738 (8th Cir. 2001) (en banc); Klein v. San Diego County, 463 F.3d 1029 (9th
104 Cir. 2006); Bell v. City of Winter Park, 745 F.3d 1318 (11th Cir. 2014). This legislation adheres
105 to the case law in the lower courts and Supreme Court upholding regulations on demonstrations
106 that target a specific residence by emphasizing both the narrowly tailored regulation to serve
107 both significant and compelling governmental interests and focusing on harassing behaviors that
108 exceed constitutional protection.

110 section 2 constitute emergency circumstances, making it necessary that the “Residential
111 Tranquility Emergency Amendment Act of 2025” be adopted after a single reading.
112 Sec. 4. This resolution shall take effect immediately.
