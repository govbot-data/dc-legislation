10 IN THE COUNCIL OF THE DISTRICT OF COLUMBIA

15 To recognize and celebrate FRESHFARM FoodPrints, on the occasion of its 20th anniversary, for
16 its exceptional food and garden education programming for District of Columbia schools
17 and students.
18
19 WHEREAS, FRESHFARM FoodPrints, a program of the non-profit FRESHFARM, began
20 at Rudolph Elementary School in Ward 4 in 2005, and then Watkins Elementary School in Ward 6
21 in 2009;
22
23 WHEREAS, FRESHFARM has built a unique and successful model of immersive food
24 and garden education, putting the District on the map as a leader in this field;
25
26 WHEREAS, FoodPrints has grown to reach 8,000 students in 21 partner DC public
27 elementary schools in 7 wards across the District;
28
29 WHEREAS, the FoodPrints Grow, Cook, Eat, Learn model embeds food educators in
30 public schools, focused on historically underserved communities, with immersive, academically
31 aligned, joyful experiences around food;
32
33 WHEREAS, over the past 20 years, FoodPrints has developed an evidence-based unique
34 curriculum containing 63 standards-based lessons, all tied to academic standards, sequenced
35 seasonally, and adaptable for a range of uses, from garden- and culinary-based programs, to
36 supplemental classroom learning, to out-of-school time enrichment programming;
37
38 WHEREAS, local food is a cornerstone of FoodPrints’ students’ experience - FoodPrints’
39 21 school gardens grow 20 different crops each school year, from strawberries to kale to sweet
40 potatoes, augmented by tens of thousands of pounds of produce from mid-Atlantic farmers in the
41 FRESHFARM network;
42
43 WHEREAS, students learn where food comes from and how to harvest and cook the food;
44
45 WHEREAS, FoodPrints built its first teaching kitchen at Watkins Elementary School in
46 2011, thanks to a fundraiser championed by chef and philanthropist José Andrés;
1

47
48 WHEREAS, since then, together with DC Public Schools, FoodPrints has added 7 beautiful
49 teaching kitchens as part of school modernizations in 6 wards across the city;
50
51 WHEREAS, in February 2016, First Lady Michelle Obama made a surprise visit, filmed
52 by HGTV, to a fifth-grade FoodPrints class at the Watkins Elementary teaching kitchen as part of
53 her Let's Move! Initiative - the First Lady helped the students grind corn to make tortillas and make
54 kale salad with greens from the school garden;
55
56 WHEREAS, research conducted by The George Washington University Milken Institute
57 School of Public Health has demonstrated that the program has made lasting impacts on culinary
58 confidence and food choices, and FoodPrints students eat more fruits and vegetables than their
59 typical peers;
60
61 WHEREAS, a generation of students has participated over the years, creating a network of
62 former students who, to this day, still have fond memories of cooking and eating favorite recipes
63 together, including Apple Beet Carrot (ABC) Salad and Tuscan Kale Salad;
64
65 WHEREAS, FoodPrints reaches beyond the classroom to build community and improve
66 food access in partnership with other FRESHFARM programs through school-based farm stands
67 at Whittier Elementary School in Ward 4 and Simon Elementary School in Ward 8, where school
68 families and neighborhood residents shop for seasonal produce grown by regional farmers;
69
70 WHEREAS, both these farm stands accept SNAP/EBT, FMNP, Produce Plus, and
71 FreshMatch, making fresh fruits and vegetables affordable for all community members;
72
73 WHEREAS, the FoodPrints team has spent many non-teaching days in service at Deep
74 Roots Farm - a Black-woman-owned farm in Maryland that is part of the FRESHFARM network
75 - planting and harvesting crops;
76
77 WHEREAS, Farmer Gale of Deep Roots Farm has, over the years, visited FoodPrints
78 classrooms to share the work of a farmer, and has provided produce for classes, events, and
79 families;
80
81 WHEREAS, since 2020, FRESHFARM has used the FoodPrints approach in community
82 settings, primarily in early childhood education centers and recently for a DC-based Food is
83 Medicine program at Children’s National Hospital;
84
85 WHEREAS, in 2024, FoodPrints participated in the first-ever U.S. Department of Health
86 and Human Services Food is Medicine Conference, where students on stage demonstrated for the
87 audience how to cook FoodPrints recipes and shared their experiences in the program;
88
89 WHEREAS, FoodPrints is funded by investments from the Council, the Office of the State
90 Superintendent for Education, the Office of the Deputy Mayor for Education, individual schools
91 and parent-teacher associations, and foundations and private donations; and
92
2

93 WHEREAS, the continuation of strong partnerships and city investment can continue to
94 create community and joy around nutritious food in the District for the next 20 years and beyond.
95
96 RESOLVED, BY THE COUNCIL OF THE DISTRICT OF COLUMBIA, that this
97 resolution may be cited as the “FRESHFARM FoodPrints 20th Anniversary Recognition
98 Resolution of 2026”.
99
100 Sec. 2. The Council of the District of Columbia recognizes and celebrates FRESHFARM
101 FoodPrints, on the occasion of its 20th anniversary, for its exceptional food and garden education
102 programming for District of Columbia schools and students.
103
104 Sec. 3. This resolution shall take effect immediately upon the first date of publication in
105 the District of Columbia Register.
