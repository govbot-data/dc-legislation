Statement of Introduction
School Food Waste Reduction and Composting Act of 2026
May 7, 2026
Today, alongside Councilmembers Henderson, T. White, Allen, Pinto, and Nadeau, I am
introducing the School Food Waste Reduction and Composting Act of 2026, legislation to reduce
food waste in District schools, expand organics collection, and strengthen food donation
practices across public school meal programs.
Every school day, District students are served meals through DCPS and public charter school
food programs. While these meals are essential to student health and learning, they also generate
significant food waste. Too often, excess food and compostable material end up in the trash, even
when some of that food could be donated or recovered and the remaining organic waste could be
separated for composting. This bill helps move the District toward a more practical and
accountable system for reducing waste in schools.
The District has already established ambitious zero waste goals, but school-level implementation
has remained uneven. Schools cannot successfully compost without clear agency responsibilities,
collection infrastructure, technical assistance, and coordination with food service providers. This
legislation addresses those gaps by establishing a clearer implementation framework for school
organics collection programs.
The bill requires DCPS and public charter schools, including food service vendors operating on
their behalf, to separate food waste for organics collection beginning January 1, 2027. It assigns
the Department of Public Works responsibility for compliance guidance, technical assistance,
and enforcement, while requiring the Department of General Services to ensure the provision and
maintenance of necessary containers, signage, and collection infrastructure at District-owned or
leased school facilities. The bill also requires OSSE to coordinate with agencies, schools, and
food service providers to support implementation and reporting.
To support accountability and long-term implementation, the legislation requires the Mayor to
issue implementing rules within 180 days and establishes annual reporting requirements on
compliance trends, technical assistance, implementation barriers, and school food donation
activity.
Finally, the bill amends the Healthy Schools Act to encourage schools to establish share tables
and donate excess edible food where practicable. It also incorporates school-level food donation

reporting into existing wellness reporting systems to better track donation practices and barriers
to expanding food recovery efforts.
The School Food Waste Reduction and Composting Act of 2026 is a practical step toward
reducing waste, supporting food recovery, and making the District’s zero waste goals more
achievable in schools. I look forward to working with my colleagues, District agencies, schools,
and advocates to move this legislation forward.

29 To amend the Zero Waste Omnibus Amendment Act of 2020 and the Healthy Schools Act of
30 2010 to require food waste separation and organics collection in public school meal
31 programs, clarify agency responsibilities for implementation, ensure provision of
32 necessary infrastructure and equipment, and incorporate school-level food donation
33 reporting into existing reporting systems.
34 BE IT ENACTED BY THE COUNCIL OF THE DISTRICT OF COLUMBIA, That this
35 act may be cited as the “School Food Waste Reduction and Composting Act of 2026”.
36 Sec. 2. The Sustainable Solid Waste Management Amendment Act of 2014, effective
37 March 16, 2021 (D.C. Law 23-211; D.C. Official Code § 8-1031.01 et seq.), is amended as
38 follows:
39 (a) Section 103a(b) (D.C. Official Code 8-1031.03a(b)) is amended by adding a new
40 paragraph (2A) to read as follows:

41 “(2A) Beginning January 1, 2027, District of Columbia Public Schools and public charter
42 schools, including any food service program or vendor operating on their behalf, that prepare or
43 handle food waste onsite.”.
44 (b) Section 104a (D.C. Official Code § 8-1031.04a) is amended by adding new
45 subsections (e) and (f) to read as follows:
46 “(e)(1) DPW shall be responsible for compliance guidance, technical assistance, and
47 enforcement of organics separation requirements applicable to District of Columbia Public
48 Schools (“DCPS”) and public charter school meal programs under this act.
49 “(2) The Department of General Services shall ensure the provision, installation,
50 and maintenance of necessary containers, signage, and collection infrastructure to support
51 compliance with this section at District-owned or District-leased public school facilities and shall
52 coordinate with public charter school operators or their designees to support the provision of
53 such infrastructure at public charter school facilities.
54 “(3) The Office of the State Superintendent of Education shall coordinate with DPW and
55 the Department of General Services, and with food service providers operating on behalf of
56 schools, to support implementation within school meal programs and to facilitate the collection
57 and transmission of reporting data to DPW.
58 “(f)(1) Within 180 days after the effective date of this subsection, the Mayor shall issue
59 rules to implement this section for public school and public charter school meal programs,
60 including establishing a corrective action process for DCPS and public charter school meal
61 programs that are not in compliance with the requirements of this section, including timeframes
62 for remediation, technical assistance, and follow-up review.

63 “(2) Beginning October 1 of the first full fiscal year after the effective date of this
64 subsection, and annually thereafter, the Department of Public Works shall submit to the Council
65 a report that includes:
66 “(A) A summary of compliance and contamination trends for public
67 school and public charter school meal programs;
68 “(B) A description of technical assistance activities and corrective actions
69 undertaken during the preceding fiscal year;
70 “(C) Identification of recurring implementation barriers, including barriers
71 related to equipment, staffing, contracts, and collection logistics; and
72 “(D) A summary of school food donation activity and barriers to donation,
73 based on information provided by the Office of the State Superintendent of Education.”.
74 Sec. 3. Section 203(d) of the Healthy Schools Act of 2010, effective July 27, 2010 (D.C.
75 Law 18-209; D.C. Official Code § 38-822.03(d)), is amended as follows:
76 (a) Paragraph (1) is amended to read as follows:
77 “(1) Public schools, public charter schools, and participating private schools are
78 encouraged to establish share tables and to donate excess edible food, as practicable.”.
79 (b) Paragraph (2) is amended by striking the phrase “share table participation” and
80 inserting the phrase “share table participation, including whether the school donates excess
81 edible food, a summary of donation practices and partners, any barriers to donating excess edible
82 food, and steps taken to expand donation capacity, ” in its place.
83 (c) A new paragraph (3) is added to read as follows:
84 “(3) Information collected pursuant to this subsection shall be reported through existing
85 school health and wellness reporting systems administered by the Office of the State

86 Superintendent of Education and transmitted to the Department of Public Works for inclusion in
87 reporting in accordance with section 104a of the Sustainable Solid Waste Management
88 Amendment Act of 2014, effective March 16, 2021 (D.C. Law 23-211; D.C. Official Code § 8-
89 1031.04a).”.
