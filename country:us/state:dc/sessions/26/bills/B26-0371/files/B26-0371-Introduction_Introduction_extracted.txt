Statement of Introduction
Closing of a Portion of a Public Alley and Establishment of a Building Restriction Line in
Square 2319, S.O. 22-02364, Act of 2025
September 29, 2025
Today, I am introducing the Closing of a Portion of a Public Alley and Establishment of a
Building Restriction Line in Square 2319, S.O. 22-02364, Act of 2025—legislation to close a
long-unused paper alley that no longer serves any public purpose.
This alley, located in Square 2319, was deeded to the District in 1934. In the ninety years since,
it has never been used as a public right-of-way. Instead, it has been completely fenced in and is
indistinguishable from the adjacent private yards. It is what’s known as a “ghost alley”—a strip
of land that exists only on paper, with no history of use and no foreseeable public benefit.
The closure of this alley will resolve several issues for both the property owner and the
community. First, it will allow for the planting of buffering landscaping between the property
and Maret School’s recently leased athletic field and clubhouse, helping to preserve privacy for
neighbors as well as the school. The Episcopal Center for Children, which owns part of the land,
supports this application and has agreed to convey its two-foot share of the alley in recognition
of the burden of new school facilities so close to nearby homes.
Second, leaving this ghost alley in place creates a burden on title. When the current property
owner purchased the home, the unclear purpose and history of the alley raised questions that
complicated the sale. Closing the alley clarifies ownership and removes this unnecessary legal
encumbrance.
Third, the alley affects zoning compliance. Without including this four-foot strip, the side yard is
noncompliant with District zoning regulations. Incorporating the alley space into the property
will bring the site into compliance.
Finally, once conveyed into private ownership, the alley will generate annual property tax
revenue for the District—converting an unused strip of land into a taxable asset.
In sum, this bill addresses a unique situation by closing an alley that has never served the public,
cannot reasonably serve the public in the future, and instead burdens adjacent property owners
and the community. It allows for needed privacy landscaping, clears title, ensures zoning
compliance, and creates new tax revenue. I look forward to working with my colleagues to
advance this commonsense measure.

29 extended over the portion of the alley to be closed.
