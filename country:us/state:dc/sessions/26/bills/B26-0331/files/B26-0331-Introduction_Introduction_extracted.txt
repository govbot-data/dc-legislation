Dear Secretary Howard,
Today, I, along with Councilmember Nadeau, am introducing the “Harmony Park Designation Act
of 2025.” Please find enclosed a signed copy of the legislation.
This legislation would symbolically designate the triangle park between New Jersey Avenue NW
and Rhode Island Avenue NW as “Harmony Park.” This park sits in Ward 1, but on the border of
Ward 2 and Ward 5. The proposed name “Harmony Park” was chosen in honor of the Columbian
Harmony Society and the Columbian Harmonean Cemetery, which is located one block from the
park. The term “harmony” also serves as a fitting name for the “pleasing arrangement” or joining
of these three wards.1
The original idea for naming the park as “Harmony Park” comes from ANC 6E, now ANC 2G
after redistricting, which passed a resolution on March 1, 2022.2 ANC 1B and 2G passed
resolutions requesting the park be named “Harmony Park” on July 11, 2025.3
The Columbian Harmony Society was a mutual aid society founded in 1825 to support African
Americans in the District by supporting them through hardships and creating a community.4 In
1828, the Society established the Harmonean Cemetery in the Shaw neighborhood as a burial place
for African Americans in the District.5 Due to a new DC ordinance at the time, the cemetery was
forced to relocate.6 In 1857, the Society opened a new location in response, moving to a much
larger lot at Rhode Island Avenue and Brentwood Road.7 In the 1920s, the area that surrounded
the cemetery began to be developed with racially restrictive covenants that limited new home
purchases to white purchasers only.8 Throughout the early 1900s, the Society sold portions of the

land due to financial issues.9 In 1960, the burials were removed and reinterred at the National
Harmony Memorial Park in Maryland.10 In 1967, the cemetery was sold to the DC government by
Louis Bell, a real estate investor who had purchased the cemetery in the late 1950s.11 In the early
1970s, 2.6 acres were sold to the Washington Metropolitan Area Transit Authority.12
Today, the Rhode Island Ave-Brentwood Metro station and complexes sit on the former site of the
Harmonean Cemetery, where 37,000 Washingtonians had been laid to rest.13 During the station
construction in the late 1970s, numerous remains and coffins were recovered.14 It was assumed the
37,000 remains had been moved to Maryland as Bell promised, but Bell and the Society’s
relocation agreement for the cemetery did not require Bell to relocate the gravestones, memorials
and monuments, and the original grave markers were not retained for reinstallation at the National
Harmony Cemetery.15 In 2012 during the DC Office of Planning’s archeological review for federal
funding of the Rhode Island Row area and in 2016 during the DC Office of Planning’s survey of
the property at Visionary Square, more human remains and markers were found and reburied at
National Harmony Cemetery.16
This legislation would recognize the significance of the Columbian Harmonean Cemetery and the
role the Columbian Harmony Society played in supporting a better future for African Americans
in the District.
Should you have any questions about this legislation, please contact my Committee and Legislative
Director, Linn Groft, at lgroft@dccouncil.gov.
