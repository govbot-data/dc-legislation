Dear Secretary Howard:
Today, I am introducing the “John R. Thompson Jr. High School Designation Act of 2025”. Please
find enclosed a signed copy of the legislation.
In 2023, the District welcomed the city’s newest high school to the Palisades neighborhood in
Ward 3. As its mission statement declares, the school seeks to “cultivate the next generation of
global leaders who thrive in a safe and supportive learning environment that is conducive to
rigorous academics and a well-rounded educational experience.”
Since its founding, the school community, including students, parents, teachers, non-instructional
school staff, and nearby residents, has been discussing a permanent name for the school. They
recently selected John R. Thompson Jr., a native Washingtonian and renowned basketball player,
coach, teacher, and advocate, as an appropriate representative of the school’s mission.
John R. Thompson Jr. (1941 – 2020) was born into a working-class family in the segregated
housing projects of the Anacostia neighborhood of Southeast DC. As a youth attending a local
parochial school, he had challenges with reading and was wrongly labeled unteachable. After
transferring to Harrison Elementary, a DC public school, he overcame those challenges with the
support oof his teachers, as well as mentors from the Police Boys Club (now the Boys & Girls
Club). He later earned a scholarship to John Carroll High School, known today as Archbishop
Carroll High School. The young Thompson, standing at 6 ft 10 in, led his high school basketball
team to three consecutive city championships and was named a second-team Parade All-American.
He subsequently received a scholarship to attend Providence College in Rhode Island, graduating
in 1964 with a degree in economics. Along the way, he also became the Friars' all-time leading
scorer. He was then drafted by the Boston Celtics, where he played for two seasons and won two
championships.

Rather than continuing playing professional basketball, John Thompson felt called to be a teacher.
He moved back to DC in 1966 and earned a master’s degree in counseling and guidance from
Federal City College, now the University of the District of Columbia (UDC), and helped lead the
college’s Center for 4-H and Youth Development. During this time, he also became a guidance
counselor and head basketball coach at St. Anthony’s High School in Northeast DC. Under
Thompson, St. Anthony’s won 122 games and recorded only 28 losses. He helped many of his
players receive college scholarships and is remembered for his emphasis on developing players’
minds and character along with their athletic abilities.
John Thompson became head basketball coach at Georgetown University in 1972. Over the next
27 seasons, he led the previously unsuccessful program to newfound success. He envisioned
himself as a teacher first, using basketball as an instrument to broaden his players’ perspective of
the world and of themselves. He focused on what he called “the game behind the game,”
encouraging his players to develop their capacity to think, excel in challenging situations, and
represent themselves with confidence. Thompson famously dedicated practice time to discussions
of current affairs and was known for demanding academic excellence from his players: 97 percent
of his players who stayed four years graduated.
Thompson was also a protector and advocate for his players. When the NCAA enacted rules to
prevent student-athletes with lower test scores from receiving scholarships, Thompson protested
the change, which he believed would impede ambitious young people from lower-income
backgrounds in seeking further education. His stance garnered national attention and the support
of many fellow coaches, and the NCAA later repealed the rule. In 1988, when Thompson learned
that a local drug kingpin had been associating with members of his team, he met him in person on
campus and told him to stop engaging with his players.
Thompson’s Hoyas won seven Big East Conference regular season titles and six Big East
tournament championships. In 1984, he became the first Black coach to win an NCAA Men’s
Division I national basketball championship. As he would emphasize, he was not the first Black
coach to have the talent, he was “the first to be afforded the opportunity.” Thompson also served
as an assistant coach for the U.S. Olympic team, including winning a gold medal in the 1976
Montreal Games. He was named National Coach of the Year three times, and 26 of his players
were drafted to the National Basketball Association, including four who were later inducted into
the Naismith Memorial Hall of Fame.
Thompson was a long-time member of the National Association of Basketball Coaches (NABC)
Board of Directors and served as NABC president in 1986. He was inducted into the Naismith
Memorial Hall of Fame in 1999, later serving as a trustee of the organization, and was also inducted
into the College Basketball Hall of Fame in 2006. He served on Nike's board of directors from
1991 until he retired in 2020. He established Georgetown's "Kids and Cops" program, which
annually gave away 10,000 tickets to the school’s basketball games. In retirement, he was a
basketball commentator and hosted “The John Thompson Show,” a sports radio talk show in the
Washington, DC area. In 2000, he founded the John Thompson Charitable Foundation for the
city’s disadvantaged children. In 2013, John Thompson donated funds to keep a Boys & Girls Club
in Northwest DC open, which is now known as the Butler-Wyatt-Thompson Jr. Boys and Girls
Clubhouse. In addition to his bachelor’s and master’s degrees, John Thompson received honorary
degrees from Georgetown University, St. Peter's College, Wheeling College, and UDC. He held
the titles of Coach Emeritus and Presidential Consultant for Urban Affairs at Georgetown

University and received the President's Award and the Patrick Healy Award, the two highest
awards which Georgetown University can confer.
I agree with the school community that John R. Thompson Jr. is an inspiring and apt namesake for
an emerging public high school in the District of Columbia, which, as the school’s official vision
statement says, aims to provide high-quality instruction, uphold inclusive values, and set the
standard in developing global leaders in society.
Should you have any questions about this legislation, please contact my Legislative Assistant, Nora
Charles, at ncharles@dccouncil.gov.
Sincerely,
Councilmember Matthew Frumin, Ward 3
Chairperson, Committee on Human Services
