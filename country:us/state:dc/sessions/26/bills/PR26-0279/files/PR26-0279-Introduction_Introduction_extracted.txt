17 To declare the existence of an emergency with respect to the need to amend the Language Access
18 Act of 2004 to include the DC Water and Sewer Authority as a covered entity with major
19 public contact; to amend the District of Columbia Public Works Act of 1954 to require that
20 the DC Water and Sewer Authority submit information regarding disconnections to the
21 Council, the Office of the Attorney General, and the Office of the Tenant Advocate on a
22 monthly basis; to amend the Water and Sewer Authority Establishment and Department of
23 Public Works Reorganization Act of 1996 to clarify the imposition of charges and penalties
24 for late payment of water service charges and bills; and to amend section 431 of Title 21 of
25 the District of Columbia Municipal Regulations to modify the notice requirements to
26 building occupants prior to a water service disconnection.
27
28 RESOLVED, BY THE COUNCIL OF THE DISTRICT OF COLUMBIA, That this
29 resolution may be cited as the “DC Water and Sewer Authority Billing and Disconnection
30 Clarification Emergency Declaration Resolution of 2025”.
31 Sec. 2. (a) In 1954, Congress enacted the District of Columbia Public Works Act of 1954
32 (“PWA”). Sections 102 and 210 of the PWA authorized charging 10% for late payment of water
33 and sewer charges, respectively, remaining unpaid after 30 days.
34 (b) In 1990, the Council enacted the District of Columbia Water and Sewer Operations
35 Amendment Act of 1990, which, among other things, amended those sections of the PWA to add
36 a late penalty of 1% per month compounded monthly for any water and sewer charges remaining
37 unpaid after 60 days.
38 (c) In 1996, the Council passed the Water and Sewer Authority Establishment and

39 Department of Public Works Reorganization Act of 1996, effective April 18, 1996 (D.C. Law 11-
40 111; D.C. Official Code § 34-2201.01 et seq.), which repealed the statutory provision for late
41 payment of water charges in section 102 of the PWA, but did not repeal the statutory provision for
42 late payment of sewer charges in section 210 of the PWA.
43 (d) Still, Section 216(d) of the PWA granted DC Water broad authority to “impose
44 additional charges and penalties for late payment of bills.” Relying on that legal authority, on
45 August 4, 2003, DC Water approved amendments to 21 DCMR § 112 that established a late penalty
46 fee of 10% for charges remaining unpaid after 30 days and late fee of 1% compounded monthly
47 for charges remaining unpaid after 60 days. There is now a legal dispute as to whether the D.C.
48 Official Code conferred DC Water with the requisite legal authority to initiate this change to the
49 DCMR.
50 (e) If the authority delegated to DC Water under Section 216(d) is interpreted as not
51 conferring DC Water with the requisite legal authority, the DC Water would only be authorized to
52 issue late fees and penalties in connection with sewer service arrearages. This effectively prohibits
53 DC Water from charging any late fees that encourage the timely payment of water charges and
54 other related services such as payment-in-lieu of taxes and right-of-way fees. Additionally, while
55 Section 210 sets a limit on the late fees that can be imposed for unpaid sanitary sewer service
56 charges, Section 216(d) includes no such limit on the imposition of late fees related to unpaid water
57 service charges.
58 (f) Separately, DC Water is also faced with $35.5 million in unpaid water bills owed to it
59 by ratepayers. In response, in March of this year, DC Water launched a more robust campaign
60 to collect outstanding balances from ratepayers, issuing its first of many notices of disconnection
61 to housing providers of multifamily apartment buildings. The notices advised tenants residing at
2

62 the properties that the water service would be terminated unless housing providers addressed
63 their outstanding water bills.
64 (g) As of May 21, 2025, a total of 331 properties had received a notice of potential water
65 service disconnection: 327 small multi-unit buildings (ranging from 4 to 6 units on average) and
66 4 larger apartment buildings. Of those 331 properties, 42 had their water service disconnected,
67 and as of May 30, 2025, 24 remained disconnected. Since June 1, 2025, an additional 29
68 properties have been identified for disconnection.
69 (h) DC Water’s recent efforts at collecting outstanding debts have revealed significant
70 challenges associated with water shutoffs at multifamily residential buildings. First, in some of
71 these cases, tenants have been paying their rent consistently and did not know that their building
72 was in arrears until they saw a notice of disconnection or experienced a disconnection. In a sense,
73 these tenants are also being punished for their building owner or manager’s failure to pay the
74 water bill. Additionally, because DC Water is rarely in contact with individual tenants, they may
75 not know if a unit for which they are threatening disconnection is the home of a senior, child, or
76 a person with a disability—populations that are especially vulnerable during water service
77 shutoffs. And without more information about individual occupants at these buildings, it has
78 been difficult for District agencies (like the Office of the Tenant Advocate) or legal aid providers
79 to contact families who have experienced or are facing a disconnection to provide them with
80 assistance or guidance.
81 (i) Requiring that DC Water provide monthly updates to the Council, the Office of the
82 Attorney General (“OAG”), and the Office of the Tenant Advocate (“OTA”) regarding its
83 disconnection activities will better enable OAG and OTA to assist residents experiencing
84 disconnection, and will allow the Council to make more informed decisions regarding potential
3

85 changes to the disconnection process.
86 (j) Emergency legislation is therefore necessary to clarify the Council’s intent that DC
87 Water has had the authority to assess late payment fees on water service arrearages since April
88 18, 1996, and that those fees are subject to the same limitations as fees for sewer service
89 arrearages. Emergency legislation is also necessary to require that DC Water submit monthly
90 reports to the Council, OAG, and OTA regarding its disconnection activities.
91 Sec. 3. The Council determines that the circumstances enumerated in section 2 constitute
92 emergency circumstances making it necessary that the DC Water and Sewer Authority Billing
93 and Disconnection Clarification Emergency Amendment Act of 2025 be adopted after a single
94 reading.
95 Sec. 4. This resolution shall take effect immediately.
