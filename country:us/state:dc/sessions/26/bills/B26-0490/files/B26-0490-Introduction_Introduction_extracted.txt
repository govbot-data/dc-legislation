Today, along with Councilmembers Charles Allen, Robert C. White, Jr., Brooke Pinto, Brianne K. Nadeau,
Wendell Felder, Janeese Lewis George, Christina Henderson, and Zachary Parker, I am introducing the Library
E-book Pricing Fairness Amendment Act of 2025.
Access to information is a fundamental right, and libraries are one of the District’s most important tools for
ensuring that right is realized. Yet, the increasing shift from physical to electronic materials has placed new
pressures on our libraries and the residents they serve. Demand for eBooks and audiobooks has surged
nationally in the last five years, 1 with the District seeing an above average increase, rising 18% between Fiscal
Year (FY) 23 and FY24.2
Libraries have struggled to keep up with demand in part because of restrictive practices on the part of
publishers. These practices make books more expensive, limit how many books libraries can (or are allowed
to) place in circulation, and ultimately drive up wait times for readers. Publishers use their leverage in
bargaining to impose steep price increases on new and popular titles. They also restrict lending directly by
imposing license repurchase requirements and time-based loan restrictions. Publishers, for instance, can time-
limit licenses to require libraries to purchase licenses over and over again, at a premium each time. As a result,
libraries are paying more to have fewer books in circulation. In FY24, eBook prices rose from an average of
$60 per license to $75, and audiobooks jumped from $85 to $120 per license.3 At the same time, wait times
have grown: the average hold-to-copy ratio for print titles tripled between FY23 and FY24,4 and average hold
wait times for eBooks increased from 34 days in FY22 to 36 days in FY24.5 These trends leave residents facing
longer delays, reduced access, and a system increasingly forced to choose between maintaining a robust print
collection and meeting the growing demand for electronic literary materials.

This legislation ensures that the District of Columbia Public Library (DCPL, or the “Library”) can get more
electronic literary materials into the hands of readers at fairer prices. This bill joins a growing momentum with
other jurisdictions6 to respond to the unfair trade practices of the largest publishing houses, requiring them to
negotiate with libraries in good faith.
Specifically, this legislation will prohibit the Library from entering into or renewing licensing agreements with
publishers if those agreements contain terms that restrict public access to books. The bill addresses contract
provisions that limit the number of times an item can be loaned, inflate costs beyond what the public pays, or
restrict the Library’s ability to preserve electronic materials for future use. Critically, the bill is structured to
take effect only once 10 other jurisdictions with a combined population of at least 50 million have enacted
substantially similar laws. This provision ensures that the District is part of a national strategy for fair electronic
access while minimizing the risk of legal challenge or market isolation.
I look forward to continuing my work with my colleagues, DCPL, and District residents to ensure that libraries
can adapt to an increasingly digital world without compromising equitable access to literature and knowledge.
