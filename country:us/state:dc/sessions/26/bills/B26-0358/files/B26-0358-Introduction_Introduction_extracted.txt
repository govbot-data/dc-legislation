Dear Secretary Howard,
Today, I, along with Chairman Phil Mendelson and Councilmembers Brianne Nadeau, Anita
Bonds, Matthew Frumin, Kenyan McDuffie, and Wendell Felder, am introducing the First
Responder Retention Efforts Amendment Act of 2025. Please find enclosed a signed copy of the
legislation.
Over the last several years, maintaining appropriate staffing levels across the District’s public
safety agencies has been a challenge. Both the Fire and Emergency Medical Services Department
(“FEMS”) and the Metropolitan Police Department (“MPD”) have struggled with short staffing
and significant overtime pressures in recent years. Staffing shortages within fire and police
departments has become a major issue across the Metropolitan area, which puts the public safety
of District residents and visitors at risk. While recruitment and retention levels within MPD have
improved to about 3,200 officers as of August of 2025, MPD Chief Pamela Smith says an ideal
staffing level would be closer to 4,000.
This legislation would expand the MPD Senior Officer Program, which permits MPD to rehire
retired police officers on annual contracts under specific circumstances, to include lieutenants in
addition to the officers who currently qualify for this program. Currently, the MPD Senior Officer
Program allows certain officers, detectives, and sergeants who are retired to be rehired at specific
pay scales at the discretion of the Chief of Police without jeopardy to their retirement benefits.
Those who qualify, however, do not currently include lieutenants, and thus this legislation would
expand the MPD Senior Officer Program to allow lieutenants to also be rehired under these criteria.
The Senior Officer Program is a useful tool to help ensure that MPD maintains experienced
employees while mitigating the retirement bubble. The program assigns the Senior Police Officers
to technical, administrative, or special skills positions in accordance with their respective
knowledge, skills, and abilities, and thus expanding the program to lieutenants would open up a
group of officers who have unique skills that can continue to contribute to the mission of MPD.
1

This legislation would also eliminate the mandatory retirement age of 60 years for both MPD
police officers and Fire and EMS workers, ensuring that the agencies do not prematurely lose
skilled and experienced members.
These changes will help Fire and EMS and MPD retain their most experienced members for longer,
mitigate the retirement bubble, and better anticipate future staffing needs. This is crucial.
Continued support and investment from the government is needed to ensure the stability of the
Departments and their ability to retain experienced police officers and firefighters, and to allow
those individuals to use their experience and expertise to train new officers and firefighters.
Thank you,
Brooke Pinto

1 To amend the Retired Police Officer Redeployment Amendment Act of 1992 to expand the
2 Metropolitan Police Department Senior Officer Program to Metropolitan Police
3 Department Lieutenants, and to amend, on an emergency basis, the Policemen and
4 Firemen's Retirement and Disability Act to remove the Metropolitan Police Department
5 and Fire and Emergency Medical Services mandatory retirement age.
6
7 BE IT ENACTED BY THE COUNCIL OF THE DISTRICT OF COLUMBIA, That this
8 act may be cited as the “First Responder Retention Efforts Amendment Act of 2025”.
9 Sec. 2. The Retired Police Officer Redeployment Amendment Act of 1992, effective
10 September 29, 1992 (D.C. Law 9-163; D.C. Official Code § 5-761(d)), is amended to read as
11 follows:
1

12 “(d) A retired police officer who is rehired under subsection (a) of this section as a:
13 “(1) Senior police officer shall be paid a salary of no more than that equal to the
14 salary paid a Class 1, Step 5 Officer and shall not be eligible for longevity pay; and
15 “(2) Senior police lieutenant shall be paid a salary of no more than that equal to
16 the salary paid a Class 5, Step 3 lieutenant and shall not be eligible for longevity pay.”.

19 follows:
20 “(b) Any member of the United States Secret Service Uniformed Division or of the

22 the age of 60 years, to whom this subchapter apply shall, in the discretion of the head of the
23 member’s department, be retired from the service and shall be entitled to receive an annuity as
