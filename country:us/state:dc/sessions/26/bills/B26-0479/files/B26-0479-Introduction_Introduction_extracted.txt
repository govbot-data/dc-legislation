Dear Secretary Howard,
Today, along with Councilmembers Brianne Nadeau, Kenyan McDuffie, Wendell Felder, and
Zachary Parker, I am introducing the “Youth Villages Grant Establishment Amendment Act of
2025.” Far too many of our young people, especially those who have experienced contact with the
justice system, struggle to find stable support networks in and throughout their communities.
Additionally, existing services and supports for young people in the District are often fragmented,
short-term, or inaccessible to youth who need sustained guidance, daily mentorship, and prolonged
community belonging.1
This bill adapts a proven local model, DC’s Senior Villages, and applies it to our youth population
to offer the same level of daily assistance and community networking that we offer to our seniors.
Villages are neighborhood-based, nonprofit membership organizations that rely on volunteer and
staff support to deliver day-to-day assistance, social and educational programming, and
connections to service providers, allowing members to remain engaged and supported within their
own communities.2 Villages are run as grassroots organizations that coordinate services, recruit
volunteers, and provide social programming and practical help.3 These characteristics, hyper-local
focus, membership orientation, volunteer mobilization, and sustained relationship-building, are
precisely the mechanisms Youth Villages will employ to keep young people connected to supports
they will actually use.
The Youth Villages Grant Establishment Amendment Act creates a new grant program within the
Child and Family Services Agency (CFSA) to build neighborhood-based, intergenerational
networks known as Youth Villages. The Youth Villages will function as local hubs for mentorship,
skill-building, civic engagement, and community reintegration for youth up to age 25 who are
justice-involved or at risk of justice involvement.
Specifically, this legislation would:

• Establish a Youth Villages grant program within CFSA to support creation and operation
of neighborhood-based organizations across DC;
• Provide grants of up to $300,000 to eligible nonprofit organizations beginning in Fiscal
Year 2027 to develop Youth Villages that serve individuals up to age 25 who are justice-
involved or at high risk of justice involvement;
• Require each Youth Village to provide daily support including mentorship, peer
leadership, intergenerational engagement, academic and career skills training, civic
engagement, community service, arts and culture, life-skills development, and
connections to food, travel, and housing assistance;
• Build sustainable neighborhood-based intergenerational networks through partnerships
with local schools, juvenile justice agencies, community organizations, businesses, and
faith-based partners; and
• Demonstrate measurable outcomes including reductions in recidivism, increases in school
attendance and graduation, employment and training enrollment, civic and community
engagement, and positive youth identity and perception, with a focus on equity and
serving historically underserved youth and neighborhoods.
The bill’s competitive grant structure, CFSA support, performance reporting, and emphasis on
intergenerational mentorship and measurable outcomes mirror the governance and sustainability
features that have allowed DC’s Senior Villages to operate successfully, while tailoring services
to the distinct needs of youth, including mentorship, employment pathways, academic supports,
crisis assistance, and civic engagement. In the District today, where young workers experience
elevated unemployment and youth referred for treatment endure growing wait times, this
neighborhood-led, wraparound model is a pragmatic complement to system-based reforms.4
Should you have any questions about this legislation, please contact my Senior Legislative Policy
Advisor, Anaiah Mitchell, at amitchell@dccouncil.gov.

29 To amend the Prevention of Child Abuse and Neglect Act of 1977 to establish a grant program
30 within the Child and Family Services Agency to support the creation and operation of
31 Youth Villages across the District of Columbia, and to provide grant funding to nonprofit
32 organizations to develop neighborhood-based, intergenerational networks to provide
33 daily supports to youth up to age 25, particularly those who are justice-involved or at risk
34 of justice involvement, promote positive community engagement and leadership
35 development among youth, and strengthen connections between generations through
36 mentorship, community service, and skill-building activities.
37
38 BE IT ENACTED BY THE COUNCIL OF THE DISTRICT OF COLUMBIA, That this
39 act may be cited as the “Youth Villages Grant Program Establishment Amendment Act of 2025”.
40 Sec. 2. Section 310 of The Prevention of Child Abuse and Neglect Act of 1977, effective
41 September 11, 2019 (D.C. Law 23-16; D.C. Official Code § 4-1303.10) is amended as follows:

42 (a) The existing text is designated as subsection (a).
43 (b) A new subsection (b) is added to read as follows:
44 “(b)(1) There is established within the Child and Family Services Agency the Youth
45 Villages Program to regularly provide programming and assist with and check on participating
46 youths’ physical, mental, and emotional well-being.
47 “(2)(A) Notwithstanding the Grant Administration Act of 2013, effective
48 December 24, 2013 (D.C. Law 20-61; D.C. Official Code § 1-328.11 et seq.), in Fiscal Year
49 2027, the Agency shall solicit applications, award, administer, monitor, and evaluate grants of up
50 to $300,000 to eligible applicants located within the District of Columbia for the purposes of
51 establishing the Youth Villages Program in the District.
52 “(B) Grant funds may be used for operations, staffing, maintenance,
53 programming, and infrastructure improvements.
54 “(3) Each Youth Village grantee shall:
55 “(A) Engage and support individuals up to 25 years of age who are justice-
56 involved or at high risk of justice involvement, with a focus on equity and serving historically
57 underserved youth and neighborhoods;
58 “(B) Provide daily support to members of the Village, including
59 mentorship, peer leadership, intergenerational engagement, academic and career skills training,
60 civic engagement, community service, arts and culture, life-skills development, food and travel
61 assistance, temporary or emergency housing or shelter assistance, and connection to evidence-
62 informed practices that reduce negative perceptions of youth and promote positive trajectories;
63 “(C) Provide daily support to members of the Village, including
64 mentorship, peer leadership, intergenerational engagement, academic and career skills training,
65 civic engagement, community service, arts and culture, life-skills development, food and travel

66 assistance, temporary or emergency housing or shelter assistance, and connection to evidence-
67 informed practices that reduce negative perceptions of youth and promote positive trajectories;
68 “(D) To the extent possible, make available the services and supports
69 outlined in this section to eligible staff and volunteers;
70 “(E) Build sustainable neighborhood-based intergenerational networks and
71 facilitate community engagement and partnerships with local schools, juvenile justice agencies,
72 community organizations, businesses, and faith-based partners; and
73 “(F) Demonstrate measurable outcomes, including reductions in recidivism,
74 increases in school attendance, high school graduation, employment, job or skills training
75 enrollment, matriculation into higher education, civic/community engagement, positive identity
76 and perception of youth, and community reintegration.
77 “(3) Eligible applicants shall be registered nonprofit corporations or associations that
78 have applied for incorporation through the Internal Revenue Service and the D.C. Office of Tax
79 and Revenue and are located in the District of Columbia.
80 “(4) For the purposes of this subsection, the term:
81 “(A) “Member” means an individual under 25 years of age who enrolls in the
82 program and currently is or has been in contact with the juvenile justice or criminal justice
83 system, including arrest, diversion, probation, incarceration, or re-entry, or an individual under
84 age 25 who is at high risk of justice involvement as determined by risk and protective factor.
85 “(B) “Youth Village” means a neighborhood-based nonprofit membership-based
86 organization, operated by a grantee under this program, that engages youth up to age 25 who are
87 justice-involved or at high risk of justice involvement in structured programming, peer and adult
88 mentoring, intergenerational activities, community service, education/employment linkage,
89 social support, and other daily services as needed.

90 “(5) The Agency shall monitor grantee performance through periodic reporting, site
91 visits, and review of performance metric data. Grantees shall submit to the Agency, at intervals
92 determined by the Agency (but at least annually), a report that includes:
93 “(A) A narrative description of program activities;
94 “(B) Quantitative data on performance indicators related to reductions in
95 recidivism (or justice-system involvement rates if applicable), job or skills training
96 enrollment, matriculation into higher education, civic/community engagement, positive
97 identity and perception of youth, and community reintegration;
98 “(C) Quantitative data regarding the number of youth served, demographic data,
99 volunteer hours, mentoring matches, school and employment outcomes (such as increases
100 in school attendance, high school graduation), referrals to DC agencies, and community
101 service hours;
102 “(D) A financial report including how grant funds were expended and any
103 leveraged funds; and
104 “(E) A description of sustainability activities and future plans.
105 “(6) The Agency shall compile an annual summary report for the Council and the Mayor
106 on the Program, including the total number of grants awarded, service areas served, youth
107 outcomes, lessons learned, and recommendations for policy/practice improvements.”.
